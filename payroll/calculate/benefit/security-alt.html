Security (stock) options

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may grant options  to your employees to buy securities as a form of compensation to encourage their greater involvement in the corporation.

Generally, there are no tax consequences to your employee at the time the option is granted.

Common lifespan of a stock option

Grant

The stock option is made. Details of the stock options are providing in a grant  agreement.

You, as the employer or payer, grant your employees the right to buy number of shares at a specific price/share (exercise price). The stock option agreement contains the start and expiring date, and other important information.

Vesting

Ability to buy and own share's contingent on vesting .

Generally, your employee can first exercise stock options when the shares begin to vest. However, some startups will allow the early exercise of unvested stock options. If your employee leaves your business before the shares vest, your employee loses their right to exercise the option because your employee never earned the right to begin with.

Exercise

Exercising the stock options is when your employee buys the stock at the exercise price.

If your employee decides to exercise, they have to pay the price based on the number of shares to buy the stock.

Sale

You may be able to sell stock immediately after exercise or in the future for a profit.

If your employee exercise their stock options, they may decide to do a same-day sale or hold the stock. Generally, your employee has the ability to control the timing of the sale. For example, if your business is acquired, your employee may likely will not have a decision about what happens to their stock.

Taxes

Depending on the type of options you provided to your employee, the benefit may be taxable at exercise and when your employee sells their stock for a profit.

Determine if a stock option agreement was made

Generally, when you grant options to your employee, a written stock option agreement exist between you (as a qualifying person (a corporation or a mutual trust fund)) and your employee.

View example of a written stock option agreement 

If there is no written stock agreement, you may find the terms for issuing securities, stock and trust units in the financial records of a corporation (for example: a directors' resolution in the corporate minutes or notes to financial statements) to determine the employment status of the person receiving the benefit.

  • If no stock option agreement exist or there is no intention in the agreement for the shares to be issued, the benefit is taxable under section 5(1) or 6(1)(a).

  • If a stock option agreement exist, the benefit may be taxable under section 7.

Determine if the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employment

To determine if the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employement, you need to determine if there is an agreement between:

  • You as a qualifying person (corporation or a mutual trust fund), including if you are in one of the following situation:

    You are a non-resident corporation

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person who is a non-resident corporation and your employee is working in Canada (or employed in Canada by a corporation with which the non-resident corporation does not deal at arm’s length), the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7.

    Example

    On March 7, an employee is granted 100 options to purchase shares of their employer's parent corporation at the FMV of $10 US ($1 US = $1.35 CDN).

    On September 2 the employee informs their employer that they wish to exercise their 100 options and pays the employer $1000 US ($1 US = $1.25 CDN). The exercise price is $10 US and the FMV is $20 US on this date.

    On September 5 the treasury shares are transferred to the employee at a FMV of $19 US ($1 US = $1.20 CDN).

    You are using a trustee as an intermediary

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person who is using a truste as an intermediary and the person receiving the benefit is your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(2).

    Example
    Description

    Ashley is granted 10,000 options on March 4, 2014, when the FMV of each share is $20. Ashley must be an employee for at least three years after date of exercise before the shares are fully vested, or the employer may buy back the shares for $0.01 each. Ashley exercises her option on May 1, 2015, when the FMV of each share is $25. In 2015, Ashley's benefit is equal to $50,000 [10,000 x (25-20)], and this amount is reported on her T4.

    On June 1, 2016, Ashley leaves the company, and as a result, forfeits her securities, and receives $100 in consideration for the $50,000 of shares. There are no changes to Ashley's T4 for 2014, 2015, or 2016, but when Ashley files her tax return, she may, in the year in which she forfeited the securities, claim a deduction of $50,000 from her income.

  • The person receiving the benefit is in one of the following situation:

    Your employee

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and the person is your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7.

    Person ceased to be your employee

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and the person ceased to be your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(4).

    Example
    Description
    • A person had the option to purchase 10,000 shares of ABC Inc. when the employment began.
    • On November 30, 2020, the person employment ceased when they resigned from his position as Manager.
    • A clause in the stock option agreement allowed the person to purchase these shares within a period of 90 calendar days after the date of termination.
    • On January 20, 2021, the person chose to purchase 20,000 shares at the price of $.50 cents per share.
    Result

    Although the person's employment ceased on November 30, 2020, any benefit from the purchase of shares in ABC Inc. on January 20, 2021 is considered taxable under subsection 7(1).

    Explanation

    If a person was your employee at the time of receiving a right to acquire shares, but subsequently ceases to be your employee (for example, change jobs or retire) before the exercise or transfer of that right, the person is treated as your employee until the transaction is completed. The benefit is included in their employment income and the person is deemed to have received the benefit as if they are still employed.

    Your employee is also a shareholder

    Not by virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and your employee is also a shareholder, the benefit is not considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(5) if all of the following apply:

    • Benefit is a distribution of profits
    • Similar benefits are not available to employees who were not shareholders
    • Duties of other employees not equally important
    Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment
    Description
    • A person is president of ABC Inc.
    • They own 51% of that corporation's common voting shares.
    • The person's spouse owns 10%.
    • The person and his spouse are the only people with a stock option agreement from ABC Inc.
    • The plan was not offered to any other employee.
    Result

    The person is in a position of control. The person and the spouse receive the benefit because of his capacity as a shareholder.

    This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

    Factors to consider to determine if the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee

    If your employee is also a shareholder, it is a question of fact whether the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee.

    You need to determine if the benefit resulted from the individual's role as an employee or their role as a shareholder. In addition to control, other factors must be considered.

    Factors to be considered based on court cases includes:

    • Was the benefit given to the taxpayer pursuant to a worker agreement with the corporation?
    • Was the benefit given pursuant to a formal agreement with the corporation?
    • Was the benefit given to reimburse costs of fees incurred by the taxpayer?
    • Was the same benefit offered to other workers, officers or directors?
    • Was the stock options or discretionary dividend shares?
    • Did clients of the corporation receive the same benefit?
    • Did the taxpayer owned 50% or more of the corporation's shares?
    • Did the corporation receive fair market value for the benefit from the taxpayer?
    • Does the benefit relate to the performance of the taxpayer’s duties at the corporation?
    • Was the benefit given to entertain clients or potential clients?
    • Did the corporation give the benefit to pay off a debt owed to the taxpayer?

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

    Self-employed person

    Not by virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and a self-employed person, this is not considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(5).

    Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment
    Description
    • A person is a self-employed consultant.
    • The person is contracted on a short-term basis by a junior public mining corporation to raise capital.
    • Part of the contract, the person was offered participation in the corporation's stock option benefit plan with details outlined in a stock option agreement.
    Result

    The person was not an employee of the junior public mining corporation.

    This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock option agreement does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

  • If the stock option agreement is not considered received by virtue of employment, this is not taxable as an employee benefit.

    Do not continue to next step.

    These amounts are not reported on a T4 slip. However, the shareholder benefit must be included on the person's T1 return and reported as business income.

  • If the stock option agreement is considered received by virtue of employment, the benefit is generally taxable under section 7.

Determine the event that caused a benefit

The benefit is taxable when one of the following applies:

  • Your employee acquires, purchases or receives the securities at less than the fair marker value (FMV) 
  • Your employee sells, transfers, or disposes of options rights for a financial gain

Definitions

Definitions for security options
Annual vesting limit
The total of the amount of employee stock options that may vest in an employee in a year and be eligible for the stock options deduction is subject to an annual limit of $200,000. The limit is based on the fair market value of the securities at the time the option was granted and the year in which these options could first be exercised. The amount of the benefit can be eligible for the stock options deduction.
Exercise
The term is used to describe the acquisition of securities under the terms of the option agreement but does not include the acquisition of shares under a provision of the option that allows an employee to receive shares equal in value to an increase in the value of the option.
Fair market value
The fair market value of a security at any particular time is usually equal to the value quoted on a stock exchange, if it is publicly traded. However there may be circumstances where this may not be the case. Specifically the fair market value of a security is the price that can be obtainable in an open and unrestricted market between knowledgeable, informed and prudent parties acting at arm's length where neither party is under any compulsion to purchase or sell the security.
Grant an option
The provision by an employer, or a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer, to an individual, of a right to acquire securities of the employer or of a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer.
Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan
Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan is an arrangement where the employee is compensated based on how the shares of the employer corporation perform on the open market.
Qualified donee
A qualified donee is generally a person to whom gifts can be made that qualify for the charitable donation deduction or tax credit.
Qualifying person
A qualifying person is a corporation or a mutual fund trust.
Security
A security is a share of the capital stock of a corporation or a unit of a mutual fund trust that is qualifying person.
Specified value
In most cases, the specified value of a security is the fair market value (FMV) of the security at the time the option is granted. Where there have been exchanges of options (to which subsection 7(1.4) of the Act apply or the security has been subject to stock splits or consolidations, the specified value is the initial FMV adjusted to take into account such events.
Specified shareholder
In general, a specified shareholder is a person who directly or indirectly owns 10% or more of the issued shares of any class of the capital stock of the corporation or any related corporation.
Vested
The term vested refers to the time when the employee can first exercise an option.
