Employee security (stock) options

Content has been updated to include the recent changes to the inclusion rate for capital gains. This change is effective June 25, 2024 and is identified with the tag New.

What are the changes

Beginning June 25, 2024, the inclusion rate for capital gains has been increased from 1/2 to 2/3. This led to the security options deductions under 110(1)(d) and 110(1(d.1) being reduced to 1/3. As a result of this change, your reporting obligations for employee security options for the 2024 calendar year have also changed.

Content has also been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language.

You, as the employer (the corporation or the mutual fund trust), may grant options  to your employees to acquire securities  of your corporation.

Generally, there are no tax consequences for your employee at the time  the option is granted.

Definitions for security options
Acquisition

An acquisition is considered to take place when:

  • Title to the shares passes to the employee
  • The employer has legal right to payment for the shares

Generally, the employee notifies the employer that they wish to exercise their options to acquire shares. This includes convertible preferred shares and short selling transactions.

Annual vesting limit

The total of the amount of employee stock options that may vest in an employee in a year and be eligible for the stock options deduction is subject to an annual limit of $200,000.

The limit is based on the fair market value of the securities at the time the option was granted and the year in which these options could first be exercised. The amount of the benefit can be eligible for the stock options deduction.

Exercise
The term is used to describe the acquisition of securities under the terms of the option agreement but does not include the acquisition of shares under a provision of the option that allows an employee to receive shares equal in value to an increase in the value of the option.
Fair market value (FMV)
The FMV of a security at any particular time is usually equal to the value quoted on a stock exchange, if it is publicly traded. However, there may be circumstances where this may not be the case. Specifically, the FMV of a security is the price that can be obtainable in an open and unrestricted market between knowledgeable, informed and prudent parties acting at arm's length where neither party is under any compulsion to purchase or sell the security.
Grant an option
The provision by an employer, or a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer, to an individual, of a right to acquire securities of the employer or of a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer.
Grant date
The date on which the security option agreement was made with an employee.
Option price

The option price is the amount specified in the option agreement for which the option holder can purchase the specified shares.

For publicly traded companies, the option price is most often the FMV, or market price, of the company's shares on the date the option was granted. The option price is also called the grant price or the strike price.

Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan
A phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan is an arrangement where the employee is compensated based on how the shares of the employer corporation perform on the open market.
Qualified donee
A qualified donee is generally a person to whom gifts can be made that qualify for the charitable donation deduction or tax credit.
Qualifying person
A qualifying person is a corporation or a mutual fund trust.
Security
A security is a share of the capital stock of a corporation or a unit of a mutual fund trust that is a qualifying person.
Specified value
In most cases, the specified value of a security is the FMV of the security at the time the option is granted. Where there have been exchanges of options (to which 7(1.4) of the Act applies or the security has been subject to stock splits or consolidations), the specified value is the initial FMV adjusted to take into account such events.
Specified shareholder
In general, a specified shareholder is a person who directly or indirectly owns 10% or more of the issued shares of any class of the capital stock of the corporation or any related corporation.
Security (stock) options

Security (stock) options is not a defined term under the Income Tax Act. In the employment context, it is generally understood to be a contractual arrangement between the employer and the employee under which the employer offers the employee the opportunity to purchase a specified number of shares in the company (or units in a mutual fund trust) at a specified, pre-determined price, at a specified future date, or within a specified future period. As the term option implies, the employee is under no obligation to purchase the stock being offered by the company.

Vested
It is the time when the employee can first exercise, sell or transfer the rights.
Vesting period

The time period in which the employee has acquired the rights but cannot exercise, sell, or transfer option rights.

The vesting period provides an incentive for the employee to remain with the employer.

Types of security options plans

The following are types of plans through which security options are granted by employers:

Stock options plan

Under this type of plan, an employer grants the employee the option to purchase a specified number of securities, at a pre-determined price  within a specified period.

Example

During the vesting period, the options partially vest over a five year period. Each year, 20% of the total options become vested , and can be exercised, sold or transferred.

This type of plan may be an incentive for the employee to remain with the employer.

Employee stock purchase plan (ESPP)

Under this type of plan, an employer grants the employee the option to acquire shares at a discounted price (an amount that is less than the value of the stock at the time the shares were acquired). Many ESPPs allow an employee to contribute a certain amount over a period of time and, for specified periods, the employee can purchase shares at a discount using the accumulated contributions.

Stock bonus plan

Under this type of plan, the employer agrees to give the securities at no cost to the employee.

Restricted stock plan or restricted stock units (RSU)

  • Employees acquire  securities subject to certain conditions (such as restriction from selling) during a vesting period, after which they are given full rights.
  • The differences between RSUs and standard stock options plans are:
    • RSUs have a vesting period for securities rights (after acquiring securities)
    • Standard stock options plans have a vesting period for the option rights (before acquiring securities)

  1. Determine if a security options agreement was made

    Generally, when you agree to grant security options  to your employee, a written security options agreement will be made between you, as a qualifying person  and your employee.

    For section 7 of the ITA to apply, the security options agreement must contain terms intending for securities to be sold or issued to your employee if the terms of the agreement are met.

    • If the benefit from the transaction is in one of the following situations, the benefit is taxable under sections 5(1) or 6(1)(a) of the ITA:

      • There was no stock option agreement.

      • There was no intention in the agreement for securities to be issued. For example, a phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan, which is an arrangement where your employee is compensated based on how your corporation’s shares perform on the open market. Your employee receives cash on the share value increasing and does not ever own the shares.

        You must include the benefit in your employee’s income in the taxation year in which your employee received the payment.

      Because section 7 of the ITA does not apply, your employee is not eligible for any security options deductions resulting from the security option.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Calculate the value of the benefit.

    • If there was a security options agreement and the security options agreement contains terms intending for securities to be sold or issued to your employee if the terms of the agreement are met, the benefit may be taxable under section 7 of the ITA.

      Continue to: Step 2 - Determine if the security options agreement was made because of an employment relationship.

  2. Determine if the security options agreement was made because of an employment relationship

    For section 7 of the ITA to apply, the security options agreement must have been made because of an employment relationship between you as a qualifying person  and your employee.

    This includes the following situations:

    You are a non-resident corporation

    By virtue of employment

    If the security options agreement was made between you as a qualifying person  who is a non-resident corporation and your employee is working in Canada (or employed in Canada by a corporation with which the non-resident corporation does not deal at arm’s length), a taxable benefit received from exercising the option is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7 of the ITA.

    Special situation: Foreign parent or subsidiary plan
    • Canadian subsidiary companies may offer plans where the employee can purchase securities of their non-resident parent company.
    • The non-resident company is responsible for withholding and reporting of the benefits to CRA unless the Canadian subsidiary reimburses the non-resident in respect of the benefit.
    • The plan may not meet the requirements for claiming the security options deduction in paragraph 110(1)(d), or the imposition of Canadian taxation may arise earlier than anticipated.
    • The CRA will allow the Canadian subsidiary to report the benefit on behalf of the non-resident parent. This does not, relieve the non-resident company from withholding and remitting the appropriate deductions

    You must calculate all amounts in Canadian dollars, even if they were paid in another currency.

    Learn more about the average exchange rates: Exchange rates

    You (or a group of employees) are using a trustee as an intermediary

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock options agreement was made between you as a qualifying person  and your employee, either of whom are using a trustee as an intermediary, a taxable benefit received from exercising the option is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7(2) of the ITA.

    Your former employee

    By virtue of employment

    If the security options agreement was made between you as a qualifying person  and your former employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7(4) of the ITA.

    Explanation

    If a person was your employee at the time of receiving a right to acquire shares, but subsequently ceases to be your employee (for example, changes jobs or retires) before the exercise or transfer of that right, the person is treated as your employee until the transaction is completed. This time period will depend on the terms of the security options agreement and how long the employee retains an option to acquire securities after their employment ceases. The benefit is included in their employment income and the person is deemed to have received the benefit as if they were still employed.

    Example
    Description
    • An employee was granted an option under a security options agreement to purchase 10,000 shares of ABC Inc during the course of their employment.
    • On November 30, 2020, the employee’s employment ceased when they resigned from their position as Manager.
    • A clause in the stock options agreement allowed the person to purchase the shares within a period of 90 calendar days after the date of termination.
    • On January 20, 2021, the person chose to purchase 20,000 shares at the price of $.50 cents per share.
    Result

    Although the person's employment ceased on November 30, 2020, the benefit from the purchase of shares in ABC Inc. on January 20, 2021 is considered to have been received by virtue of employment and taxable under subsection 7(4) of the ITA.

    Your employee is also a shareholder

    Not by virtue of employment

    If the security options agreement is between you as a qualifying person  and your employee who is also a shareholder, it is a question of fact as to whether the benefit is considered received in respect of, in the course of or by virtue of employment or as a shareholder.

    Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment

    Examples of where a security options taxable benefit are not considered received as a result of an employment relationship:

    • Benefit is a distribution of profits
    • Similar benefits are not available to employees who were not shareholders
    • Duties of other employees are not equally important
    Description
    • A person is president of ABC Inc.
    • They own 51% of that corporation's common voting shares.
    • The person's spouse owns 10%.
    • The person and his spouse are the only people who concluded a stock options agreement with ABC Inc.
    • The security options agreements were not offered to any other employee.
    Result

    The person is in a position of control. The person and the spouse receive the benefit because of the person’s capacity as a shareholder.

    The benefit received from exercising the option is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

    However, the benefit is still taxable as a shareholder benefit.

    If your employee is also a shareholder, it is a question of fact whether the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee.

    By virtue of employment

    If the stock options agreement was received by the shareholder in their capacity as an employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

    • If the stock options are not considered received by virtue of employment, these are not taxable as an employee benefit.

      Do not continue to the next step.

      If you are not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed for CPP/EI purposes

      Benefits received in their capacity as a shareholder are not reported on a T4 slip.

      However, the shareholder benefit must be included on the shareholder's personal income tax return and reported as business income.

    • If the stock options are considered received by virtue of employment, the resulting benefit is generally taxable under section 7 of the ITA.

  4. Determine if the benefit is taxable and when the benefit must be included in your employee's income

    The benefit from exercising a security option is taxable when one of the following applies:

    • Your employee acquires, purchases or receives the securities at less than their FMV  at the time
    • Your employee sells, transfers, or disposes of options under the security options agreement for a financial gain

    Select the event that caused the employee benefit

    The following events may trigger a taxable benefit:

    Event 1: Your employee exercises their rights and acquires the securities

    Taxable situation

    The benefit is always taxable to your employee if your employee chooses to exercise their rights under a security option agreement and acquires  the securities under sections 7(1)(a) or 7(1.1) of the ITA.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    You must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which:

    • You are a CCPC
      Your employee disposed the shares
    • You are non-CCPC
      Your employee acquired the shares
    Event 2: Your employee exchanges shares acquired from a CCPC under the security option agreement for new shares

    Non-taxable situation

    There is no taxable benefit from the transaction under section 7(1.5) of the ITA if your employee exchanges the shares of a CCPC for new shares and all of the following apply:

    • Your employee exchanges their shares acquired under a security options agreement of a CCPC for new shares of the employer corporation, a corporation with which the employer corporation does not deal at arm’s length  immediately after the disposition or exchange of the old shares, the newly amalgamated or merged corporation, or a corporation with which the newly amalgamated or merged corporation does not deal at arm's length immediately after the amalgamation
    • Your employee receives no consideration for the exchange other than the new option
    • The fair market value of the new shares immediately after the disposition or exchange is not more than the FMV of the old shares immediately before the disposition or exchange

    If all conditions above are met, the employee is deemed to not have disposed of or exchanged the security originally acquired. The new securities acquired in the transaction are deemed to be the same securities as those which were exchanged and the qualifying person  to be the same as the qualifying person who originally issued the securities.

    You do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to the next step.

    Taxable situation

    The benefit may be taxable for your employee if the transaction does not meet all of the conditions above.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    If there is a taxable benefit, you must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which the employee disposes of the original CCPC share.

    Event 3: Your employee exchanges their rights under the security option agreement for new security options rights

    Non-taxable situation

    There is no taxable benefit from the transaction under section 7(1.4) of the ITA if your employee exchanges the security options for a new option and all of the following apply:

    • Your employee exchanges their security options for new security options as a result of any type of corporate reorganization or capital restructuring within a non-arm’s length  corporate group
    • Your employee receives no consideration for the exchange other than the new option
    • Your employee does not derive an economic gain from the exchange

    If all conditions above are met, the employee is deemed to not have disposed of or exchanged the exchanged option. The new option received in the transaction is deemed to be the same option as the one which was exchanged and the qualifying person  to be the same as the qualifying person who originally issued the securities.

    You do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to the next step.

    Taxable situation

    The benefit may be taxable for your employee if the transaction does not meet all of the conditions above.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    If there is a taxable benefit, you must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which your employee exchanged their rights under the security options agreement for new security options rights.

    Event 4: You (as the employer) elect to cash out your employee’s rights under the security option agreement

    Taxable situation

    The payment is taxable as employment income under sections 5(1) or 6(1)(a) of the ITA if you (as the employer) elect to cash out the rights under the security options agreement.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    You must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which you (as the employer) cashed out your employee’s security options rights.

    Event 5: Your employee chooses to cash out their rights under the security option agreement

    Taxable situation

    The payment is taxable under sections under sections 7(1)(b) or 7(1)(b.1) of the ITA if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement
    • Your employee has the option of receiving either shares or cash
    • Your employee elects to cash out the rights

    When to include in your employee’s income

    You must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which your employee cashes out the options.

    Event 6: Your employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement and the employee does not have the option to receive either shares or cash

    Taxable situation

    The benefit from the transaction is taxable under sections under sections 7(1)(b) or 7(1)(b.1) of the ITA if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee disposes of their rights under the security option agreement
    • Your employee does not have the option to receiving either shares or cash
    • Your employee has disposed of security options rights to a third party as part of a transaction that results in an ownership change in the corporation

    When to include in your employee’s income

    You must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which your employee disposed of their security options rights.

    Event 7: Your employee transfers their rights to a non-arm's length person, and that person exercises the option to acquire the shares

    Taxable situation

    The benefit from the transaction is taxable under section 7(1)(c) of the ITA if your employee transfers their rights under a security options agreement by one or more transactions to a non-arm’s length  person and that person exercises the option.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    You must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which a person to whom your employee transferred their security options rights exercised the option to acquire the shares

    Event 8: Your employee transfers their rights to a non-arm's length person, and that person disposes of the rights

    Taxable situation

    The benefit from the transaction is taxable if:

    • Your employee has transferred security options to a non-arm’s length  person and that person (transferee) has disposed of the security options to an arm's length person under section 7(1)(d) of the ITA
    • The rights of your employee under a security option agreement have, by one or more transactions between persons not dealing at arm's length, become vested in a non-arm's length person (transferee), and the transferee subsequently transfers or disposes of the rights to an employer with which the transferee does not deal at arm's length (or to a corporation or mutual fund trust not dealing at arm's length with such an employer) under section 7(1)(d.1) of the ITA

    When to include in your employee’s income

    You must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which a non-arm's length person to whom your employee transferred their security disposed of the security options rights

    Event 9: Your employee dies holding unexercised options

    Nil taxable situation

    The value of options held by your employee after death is nil if the security options agreement provides that the options expire upon your employee's death.

    You do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to the next step.

    Taxable situation

    There is a deemed taxable benefit if your employee owns unexercised options under a security option agreement immediately before death and the agreement does not stipulate that the options expire upon the death of the employee under 7(1)(e) of the ITA.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    If there is a taxable benefit, you must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which your employee died who had unexercised options which do not expire upon death.

    Event 10: Your employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement

    Non-taxable situation

    There is no taxable benefit from the transaction under section 7(1.7) of the ITA if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee had rights to acquire shares under a security options agreement
    • Your employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement
    • Your employee did not receive consideration for the loss of the ability to exercise the option
    • Your employee did not exchange their rights for rights under a new security options agreement under sections 7(1)(b) or 7(1)(b.1) of the ITA
    Example

    Examples of this situation include:

    • Corporate mergers where the previous corporation no longer exists for your employee to exercise the option (where no new options were provided to the employee by the new corporation).
    • Situations where the option is cancelled by the employer.

    If all conditions above are met, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to the next step.

    Taxable situation

    The benefit is taxable for your employee if your employee receives consideration for not being able to exercise their option rights or exchanges their rights for rights under a new security options agreement.

    When to include in your employee’s income

    If there is a taxable benefit, you must include the benefit in your employee's income in the taxation year in which your employee receives consideration for no longer being able to exercise their security options rights

  5. Calculate the value of the benefit

    If your employee received a security options taxable benefit, the calculation of the benefit will depend on the event that caused the employee benefit.

    Situation 1: Your employee exercises their rights and acquires the securities (event 1 of step 4)

    The calculation of the security options benefit to use will depend on when your employee exercised their security options.

    • Exercised in the 2023 or an earlier calendar year or after the calendar year 2024

      For the 2023 or an earlier calendar year or after the calendar year 2024, use the following to calculate the value of the security options taxable benefit:

      • FMV of the shares or units at the time the employee acquired  them
      • minus Amount paid or to be paid to the qualifying person  by the employee for the securities
      • minus Amount your employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities
      • equals Value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 38 and box 14
      Example - Calculation

      An employee was granted the option to acquire 8,000 shares of their employer, Company Y, on August 15, 2023. The option price per share was $40, which matched the FMV of the shares at the time. The employees exercised the options the next day. The FMV per share was then $42.

      • $336,000 (8,000 x $42) is the FMV of the shares at the time the employee acquired them
      • minus $320,000 (8,000 x $40) is the amount paid or to be paid to the qualifying person by the employee for the securities
      • minus $0 because the employee did not pay anything to acquire the security options rights
      • equals $16,000 is the value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 38 and box 14

    • New Exercised in calendar year 2024

      For the calendar year 2024 only, use the following to calculate the value of the security options taxable benefit for each period in which your employee exercised their security options:

      • From January 1, 2024 to June 24, 2024

        • FMV of the shares or units at the time the employee acquired  them between January 1, 2024 and June 24, 2024
        • minus Amount paid or to be paid to the qualifying person by the employee for the securities
        • minus Amount your employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities
        • equals Value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 90 and box 14

      • From June 25, 2024 to December 31, 2024

        • FMV of the shares or units at the time the employee acquired  them betweeen June 25, 2024 and December 31, 2024
        • minus Amount paid or to be paid to the qualifying person by the employee for the securities
        • minus Amount your employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities
        • equals Value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 38 and box 14
      Example - Calculations

      An employee was granted the option to acquire 8,000 shares of their employer, Company Y, on April 15, 2024. The option price per share was $40, which matched the FMV of the shares at the time. The employee exercised half of their options the next day, when the FMV per share was $42. The employee then exercised their remaining options on July 15, 2024, when the FMV per share was $45.

      • From January 1, 2024 to June 24, 2024

        • $168,000 (4,000 x $42) is the FMV of the shares at the time the employee acquired them
        • minus $160,000 (4,000 x $40) is the amount paid to the qualifying person by the employee for the securities
        • minus $0 because the employee did not pay anything to acquire the security options rights
        • equals $8,000 is the value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 90 and box 14

      • From June 25, 2024 to December 31, 2024

        • $180,000 (4,000 x $45) is the FMV of the shares at the time the employee acquired them
        • minus $160,000 (4,000 x $40) is the amount paid to the qualifying person by the employee for the securities
        • minus $0 because the employee did not pay anything to acquire the security options rights
        • equals $20,000 is the value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 38 and box 14
    Situation 2: Your employee disposes of or transfers their rights to another person, cashes out their rights, or is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement (events 5, 6, 8 and 10 of step 4)

    The calculation of the security options benefit to use will depend on when the employee disposed of, transferred, cashed out or received consideration for not being able to exercise their options rights.

    • Benefit received in the 2023 or an earlier calendar year or after the calendar year 2024

      For the 2023 or an earlier calendar year or after the calendar year 2024, use the following to calculate the value of the security options taxable benefit:

      • Value of the consideration received for the security options rights
      • minus Amount your employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities
      • equals Value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 38 and box 14
      Example - Calculations

      An employee was granted the option to acquire 8,000 shares of their employer, Company Y, on April 15, 2023. The employee paid $500 to acquire these rights. Before the employee exercised their options rights, the employer cancelled the security options agreement and paid the employee $10,000 following the terms of the agreement as consideration for the loss of the rights.

      • $10,000 is the value of the consideration received for the security options rights
      • minus $500 is the amount the employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities
      • equals $9,500 is the value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 38 and box 14

    • New Benefit received in calendar year 2024

      For the calendar year 2024 only, use the following to calculate the value of the security options taxable benefit for each period in which your employee received a benefit from disposing of, transferring, cashing out or receiving consideration for not being able to exercise their options rights.

      • From January 1, 2024 to June 24, 2024

        • Value of the consideration received for the security options rights disposed of between January 1, 2024 and June 24, 2024
        • minus Amount your employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities
        • equals Value of the security options benefit to be included on the T4 slip using code 90 and box 14

      • From June 25, 2024 to December 31, 2024

        • Value of the consideration received for the security options rights disposed of between June 25, 2024 and December 31, 2024
        • minus Amount your employee paid to acquire the security options rights to acquire the securities

  6. Determine which security options deductions apply to the security options benefit

    If your employee received a security options taxable benefit under section 7 of the ITA, your employee may be eligible for security options benefit deductions.

    The security options deductions are not available if the agreement is not considered to have been received under section 7 (by virtue of employment).

    Deduction under 110(1)(d)

    Your employee is eligible to claim the security options benefit deduction under 110(1)(d) if all of the following apply:

    • A qualifying person  agreed to sell or issue to your employee shares of its capital stock or the capital stock of another corporation that it does not deal with at arm's length, or agreed to sell or issue units of a mutual fund trust
    • If the security is a share, it is a prescribed share (common shares, per 6204(1) of the employer corporation's capital stock, or those of a non-arm's length corporation) or if it is a unit, it is a unit of a mutual fund trust
    • Your employee dealt at arm's length with the qualifying person (corporation or mutual fund trust) right after the agreement was made
    • At the time when the agreement was made, the price of the share or unit was not less than its FMV 
    • If you (the employer) chooses not to claim the cash-out as an expense, you must make an election under subsection 110(1.1) by entering the total amount of the security option cash-out that your elected not to claim as an expense using code 86 - Security options election on the T4 slip. Your employee can claim the deduction on their T1 return (under paragraph 110(1)(d)).

    • If the qualifying person does not meet all of the above conditions, your employee is not eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d).

      However, they may be eligible for other deductions.

      • CCPC

        If you are a CCPC, continue to: Step 6c - Determine if your employee is eligible for the deduction under section 110(1)(d.1) of the ITA.

      • Non-CCPC or mutual fund trust

        If you are a non-CCPC or mutual fund trust, continue to: Step 6d - Determine if your employee is eligible for the deduction under section 110(1)(d.01) of the ITA.

    • If your employee meets all of the above conditions, your employee is eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d).

      Continue to: Calculate the total value of the benefit.

    • Deduction under 110(1)(d.1): Only applies if you are a CCPC

      Your employee is eligible to claim the security options benefit deduction under 110(1)(d.1) if all of the following apply:

      • CCPC shares disposed of in the year where your employee dealt at arm's length with the corporation
      • Employee has not disposed of the shares (otherwise than as a result of your employee's death) or exchanged them within two years after the date the taxpayer acquired them
      • Employee did not deduct an amount under 110(1)(d) of the ITA for the benefit

      • If the above conditions are not met, your employee is not eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d.1).

      • If the above conditions are met, your employee is eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d.1).

      Continue to: Calculate the total value of the benefit.

    • Deduction under 110(1)(d.01): Only applies if you are a non-CCPC

      If your employee acquires a security under an option from a non-CCPC or mutual fund trust and donates it to a qualified donee  (charitable donations of securities), your employee is eligible to claim the security options benefit deduction under 110(1)(d.01) if all of the following apply:

      • Donation must be made 30 days from the day the option is exercised
      • Within the year in which the option is exercised
        • Employee must be eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d) of the ITA
        • If the security is a share, it must be of a class listed on a prescribed Canadian or foreign stock exchange

      Under 110(2.1), a deduction under 110(1)(d.01) is available where an employee, in exercising an option, directs the appointed or approved broker or dealer to immediately sell the security and donate all or part of the proceeds of the disposition to a qualified charity. The amount of the deduction must be prorated to reflect the portion of the proceeds that are donated.

      • If your employee does not meet all of the above conditions, your employee is not eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d.01).

      • If your employee meets all of the above conditions, your employee is eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d.01).

      Continue to: Calculate the total value of the benefit.

  7. Determine if the $200,000 vesting limit impacts the security options deduction

    If your employee meets all of the conditions in step 6 and you are non-CCPC or mutual fund trust, you need to determine if the eligibility for the security options benefit will be impacted by the $200,000 vesting limit to determine what part of the security options benefit can qualify for the security options deduction under 110(1)(d).

    For security options granted on or after July 1, 2021, your employee is subject to a $200,000 annual vesting limit if the qualifying person  meets both of the following conditions:

    • It is not a CCPC
    • Has, or is part of a consolidated group that has, gross revenues of more than $500 million

    How to calculate a foreign plan if you are a non-resident employer

    When calculating the taxable benefit, the foreign stock options must be converted to the Canadian dollar equivalent.

    You can use the exchange rate (available from the company or the Bank of Canada) in effect on the dates that make up the transaction (acquisition date): the date the shares are paid for and the date when the treasury shares have been transferred to your employee.

    Where the amount paid or to be paid by an employee pursuant to the terms of a stock option agreement is expressed in terms of a foreign currency, it will not compromise your employee's eligibility for the deduction under paragraph 110(1)(d).
