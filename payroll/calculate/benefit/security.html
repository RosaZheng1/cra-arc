Security (stock) options

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You, as the employer, the corporation or the ual fund trust, may grant options  to your employees to buy securities as a form of compensation to encourage their greater involvement in your corporation.

Generally, there are no tax consequences to your employee at the time the option is granted.

Definitions for security options
Acquisition

An acquisition is considered to take place when:

  • Title to the shares passes to your employee
  • The employer has legal right to payment for the shares

Generally, your employee notifies the employer that they wishes to exercise their options to acquire shares. This includes convertible preferred shares and short selling transactions.

Adjusted Cost Base

The adjusted cost base (ACB) of shares acquired under a stock options agreement is the amount of any benefit included in computing income in respect of the acquisition of the share plus the price actually paid by the employee for the share.

Annual vesting limit

The total of the amount of employee stock options that may vest in an employee in a year and be eligible for the stock options deduction is subject to an annual limit of $200,000.

The limit is based on the fair market value of the securities at the time the option was granted and the year in which these options could first be exercised. The amount of the benefit can be eligible for the stock options deduction.

Exercise
The term is used to describe the acquisition of securities under the terms of the option agreement but does not include the acquisition of shares under a provision of the option that allows an employee to receive shares equal in value to an increase in the value of the option.
Fair market value
The fair market value of a security at any particular time is usually equal to the value quoted on a stock exchange, if it is publicly traded. However there may be circumstances where this may not be the case. Specifically the fair market value of a security is the price that can be obtainable in an open and unrestricted market between knowledgeable, informed and prudent parties acting at arm's length where neither party is under any compulsion to purchase or sell the security.
Grant an option
The provision by an employer, or a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer, to an individual, of a right to acquire securities of the employer or of a qualifying person that does not deal at arm's length with the employer.
Grant date
The date on which the security option agreement was given to an employee.
Option price

The option price is the amount specified in the option agreement for which the option holder can purchase the specified shares.

For publicly traded companies, the option price is most often the fair market value, or market price, of the company's shares on the date the option was granted. The option price is also called the grant price or the strike price.

Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan
Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan is an arrangement where the employee is compensated based on how the shares of the employer corporation perform on the open market.
Qualified donee
A qualified donee is generally a person to whom gifts can be made that qualify for the charitable donation deduction or tax credit.
Qualifying person
A qualifying person is a corporation or a ual fund trust.
Security
A security is a share of the capital stock of a corporation or a unit of a ual fund trust that is qualifying person.
Specified value
In most cases, the specified value of a security is the fair market value (FMV) of the security at the time the option is granted. Where there have been exchanges of options (to which subsection 7(1.4) of the Act apply or the security has been subject to stock splits or consolidations, the specified value is the initial FMV adjusted to take into account such events.
Specified shareholder
In general, a specified shareholder is a person who directly or indirectly owns 10% or more of the issued shares of any class of the capital stock of the corporation or any related corporation.
Stock options

stock options is not a defined term under the Income Tax Act. In the employment context, it is generally understood to be a contractual arrangement between the employer and the employee under which the employer offers the employee the opportunity to purchase a specified number of shares of stock in the company (or units in a mutual fund trust) at a specified, pre-determined price, at a specified future date, or within a specified future period. As the term option implies, the employee is under no obligation to purchase the stock being offered by the company.

Employee stock options (ESO) is the term often given to an option where its taxation is governed by section 7 of the Income Tax Act.

Vested
It is the time when the employee can first exercise, sell or transfer the rights.
Vesting period

The time period in which the employee has acquired the rights but cannot exercise, sell, or transfer option rights.

The vesting period provides an incentive for the employee to remain with the employer.

Types of security option plans

The following are types of plans granted by employers:

stock options plan

  • Employer grants the employee option right to acquire a specified number of securities, at a specified price , within a specified period.
  • Once granted, the option rights under the plan may be subject to a vesting period . These options may:
    • Vest immediately, on the grant date 
    • Vest over time
Example

During the vesting period, the options partially vest over a five year period. Each year, 20% of the total options become vested , and can be exercised, sold or transferred.

This type of plan may be an incentive for the employee to remain with the employer.

Employee stock purchase plan (ESPP)

  • An ESPP is treated the same as a stock options plan under subsection 7(1) of the ITA.
  • Employees can acquire  securities at a discounted price.
  • The employee holds the shares like an investor in the business.
Example

An employee contributes a certain amount over a period of time and at pre-specified periods, then purchases securities at a discount using the accumulated contributions.

This type of plan may be an incentive for the employee to remain with the employer.

Stock bonus plan

The employer agrees to give the securities at no cost to the employee.

Restricted stock plan or restricted stock units (RSU)

  • Employees acquire  securities subject to certain conditions (such as restriction from selling) during a vesting period, after which they are given full rights.
  • The difference between RSUs and standard stock options plans is:
    • RSUs have a vesting period for securities rights (after acquiring securities)
    • Standard stock options plans have a vesting period for the option rights (before acquiring securities)

Foreign parent/subsidiary plan

  • Canadian subsidiary companies may offer plans where the employee can purchase securities of their non-resident parent company.
  • The non-resident company is responsible for withholding and reporting of the benefits to CRA unless the Canadian subsidiary reimburses the non-resident in respect of the benefit.
  • The plan may not meet the requirements for claiming the security option deduction in paragraph 110(1)(d), or the imposition of Canadian taxation may arise earlier than anticipated.
  • The CRA will allow the Canadian subsidiary to report the benefit on behalf of the non-resident parent. This does not, relieve the non-resident company from withholding and remitting the appropriate deductions

  1. Determine if a security options agreement was made

    Generally, when you grant options to your employee, a written stock options agreement exist between you, as a qualifying person (a corporation or a mutual trust fund) and your employee.

    If there is no written stock agreement, you may find the terms for issuing securities, stock and trust units in the financial records of a corporation (for example: a directors' resolution in the corporate minutes or notes to financial statements) to determine the employment status of the person receiving the benefit.

    • If no stock options agreement exist or there is no intention in the agreement for the shares to be issued, the benefit is taxable under section 5(1) or 6(1)(a).

    • If a stock options agreement exist, the benefit may be taxable under section 7.

  2. Determine if the stock options agreement was made because of an employment relationship

    To determine if the stock options agreement was made because of an employment relashionship, you need to determine if there is an agreement between:

    • You as a qualifying person (corporation or a mutual trust fund), including if you are in one of the following situation:

      You are a non-resident corporation

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement is between a qualifying person who is a non-resident corporation and your employee is working in Canada (or employed in Canada by a corporation with which the non-resident corporation does not deal at arm’s length), the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7.

      Example

      On March 7, an employee is granted 100 options to purchase shares of their employer's parent corporation at the FMV of $10 US ($1 US = $1.35 CDN).

      On September 2 the employee informs their employer that they wish to exercise their 100 options and pays the employer $1000 US ($1 US = $1.25 CDN). The exercise price is $10 US and the FMV is $20 US on this date.

      On September 5 the treasury shares are transferred to the employee at a FMV of $19 US ($1 US = $1.20 CDN).

      You are using a trustee as an intermediary

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement is between a qualifying person who is using a truste as an intermediary and the person receiving the benefit is your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(2).

      Example
      Description

      Ashley is granted 10,000 options on March 4, 2014, when the FMV of each share is $20. Ashley must be an employee for at least three years after date of exercise before the shares are fully vested, or the employer may buy back the shares for $0.01 each. Ashley exercises her option on May 1, 2015, when the FMV of each share is $25. In 2015, Ashley's benefit is equal to $50,000 [10,000 x (25-20)], and this amount is reported on her T4.

      On June 1, 2016, Ashley leaves the company, and as a result, forfeits her securities, and receives $100 in consideration for the $50,000 of shares. There are no changes to Ashley's T4 for 2014, 2015, or 2016, but when Ashley files her tax return, she may, in the year in which she forfeited the securities, claim a deduction of $50,000 from her income.

    • The person receiving the benefit is in one of the following situation:

      Your employee

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement is between a qualifying person and the person is your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under section 7.

      Person ceased to be your employee

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement is between a qualifying person and the person ceased to be your employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(4).

      Example
      Description
      • A person had the option to purchase 10,000 shares of ABC Inc. when the employment began.
      • On November 30, 2020, the person employment ceased when they resigned from his position as Manager.
      • A clause in the stock options agreement allowed the person to purchase these shares within a period of 90 calendar days after the date of termination.
      • On January 20, 2021, the person chose to purchase 20,000 shares at the price of $.50 cents per share.
      Result

      Although the person's employment ceased on November 30, 2020, any benefit from the purchase of shares in ABC Inc. on January 20, 2021 is considered taxable under subsection 7(1).

      Explanation

      If a person was your employee at the time of receiving a right to acquire shares, but subsequently ceases to be your employee (for example, change jobs or retire) before the exercise or transfer of that right, the person is treated as your employee until the transaction is completed. The benefit is included in their employment income and the person is deemed to have received the benefit as if they are still employed.

      Your employee is also a shareholder

      Not by virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement is between a qualifying person and your employee is also a shareholder, the benefit is not considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(5) if all of the following apply:

      • Benefit is a distribution of profits
      • Similar benefits are not available to employees who were not shareholders
      • Duties of other employees not equally important
      Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment
      Description
      • A person is president of ABC Inc.
      • They own 51% of that corporation's common voting shares.
      • The person's spouse owns 10%.
      • The person and his spouse are the only people with a stock options agreement from ABC Inc.
      • The plan was not offered to any other employee.
      Result

      The person is in a position of control. The person and the spouse receive the benefit because of his capacity as a shareholder.

      This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

      Factors to consider to determine if the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee

      If your employee is also a shareholder, it is a question of fact whether the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or as your employee.

      You need to determine if the benefit resulted from the individual's role as an employee or their role as a shareholder. In addition to control, other factors must be considered.

      Factors to be considered based on court cases includes:

      • Was the benefit given to the taxpayer pursuant to a worker agreement with the corporation?
      • Was the benefit given pursuant to a formal agreement with the corporation?
      • Was the benefit given to reimburse costs of fees incurred by the taxpayer?
      • Was the same benefit offered to other workers, officers or directors?
      • Was the stock options or discretionary dividend shares?
      • Did clients of the corporation receive the same benefit?
      • Did the taxpayer owned 50% or more of the corporation's shares?
      • Did the corporation receive fair market value for the benefit from the taxpayer?
      • Does the benefit relate to the performance of the taxpayer’s duties at the corporation?
      • Was the benefit given to entertain clients or potential clients?
      • Did the corporation give the benefit to pay off a debt owed to the taxpayer?

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

      Self-employed worker

      Not by virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement is between a qualifying person and a self-employed worker, this is not considered received by virtue of employment under subsection 7(5).

      Example - Not considered received by virtue of employment
      Description
      • A person is a self-employed consultant.
      • The person is contracted on a short-term basis by a junior public mining corporation to raise capital.
      • Part of the contract, the person was offered participation in the corporation's stock options benefit plan with details outlined in a stock options agreement.
      Result

      The person was not an employee of the junior public mining corporation.

      This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is not considered to be received by virtue of employment.

      By virtue of employment

      If the stock options agreement does not meet all conditions above, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

    • If the stock options agreement is not considered received by virtue of employment, this is not taxable as an employee benefit.

      If you are not sure if the worker is an employee or self-employed for CPP/EI purposes

      You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an employee or is self-employed. You can use the CRA guidance to help you gather the facts based on your situation.

      Learn more: Employee or Self-employed

      Do not continue to the next step.

      These amounts are not reported on a T4 slip. However, the shareholder benefit must be included on the person's T1 return and reported as business income.

    • If the stock options agreement is considered received by virtue of employment, the benefit is generally taxable under section 7.

  3. Determine if you (as the employer granting the options) are a Canadian-controlled private corporation (CCPC) or not

    The corporation is a CCPC if it meets all of the following requirements at the end of the tax year:

    • It is a private corporation
    • It is a corporation that was resident in Canada and was either incorporated in Canada or resident in Canada from June 18, 1971, to the end of the tax year
    • It is not controlled directly or indirectly by one or more non-resident persons
    • It is not controlled directly or indirectly by one or more public corporations (other than a prescribed venture capital corporation, as defined in Regulation 6700 of the Income Tax Regulations)
    • It is not controlled by a Canadian resident corporation that lists its shares on a designated stock exchange outside of Canada
    • It is not controlled directly or indirectly by any combination of persons described in the three previous conditions
    • If all of its shares that are owned by a non-resident person, by a public corporation (other than a prescribed venture capital corporation), or by a corporation with a class of shares listed on a designated stock exchange were owned by one person, that person would not own sufficient shares to control the corporation
    • No class of its shares of capital stock is listed on a designated stock exchange

    If you are a CCPC or not, will determine:

    • The timing of when to include the benefit in your employee's income (step 4)
    • Whether your employee is eligible to claim option benefit deductions (step 6)

  5. Determine which security options deductions apply to the security options benefit

    Your must determine if your employee is eligible for the following option benefit deduction:

    • CCPC

      • Deduction under section 110(1)(d.1)

        Your employee is eligible to claim the option benefit deduction under section 110(1)(d.1) if all of the following apply:

        • When the agreement to sell or issue shares to your employee was concluded, the issuing or selling corporation was a CCPC
        • Employee acquired the shares after May 22, 1985
        • Employee dealt at arm's length with the corporation or any other corporation involved right after the agreement was concluded
        • CCPC shares disposed of in the year where your employee dealt at arm's length with the corporation
        • Employee has not disposed of the shares (otherwise than as a result of your employee's death) or exchanged them within two years after the date the taxpayer acquired them
        • Employee did not deduct an amount under 110(1)(d) of the ITA for the benefit

        If your employee is not eligible for the deduction under section 110(1)(d.1), they may be eligible for the deduction under section 110(1)(d).

      • Deduction under section 110(1)(d)

        Your employee is eligible to claim the option benefit deduction under section 110(1)(d) if all of the following apply:

        • The security is a prescribed share (common shares, per 6204(1) of the employer corporation's capital stock, or those of a non-arm's length corporation) or a unit of a mutual fund trust
        • Your employee dealt at arm's length with the qualifying person (corporation or mutual fund trust) right after the agreement was made
        • At the time when the agreement was made, the price of the share or unit was not less than its FMV

    • Non-CCPC or mutual fund trust

      • Deduction under section 110(1)(d)

      • Deduction under section 110(1)(d.01)

        If your employee acquires a security under an option from a non-CCPC and donates it to a qualified donee  (charitable donations of securities) they are also eligible to claim the option benefit deduction under section 110(1)(d.01) if all of the following apply:

        • Donation must be made 30 days from the day the option is exercised
        • Within the year in which the option is exercised
          • Employee must be eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d) of the ITA
          • If the security is a share, it must be of a class listed on a prescribed Canadian or foreign stock exchange

        Under 110(2.1), a deduction under 110(1)(d.01) is available where an employee, in exercising an option, directs the appointed or approved broker or dealer to immediately sell the security and donate all or part of the proceeds of the disposition to a qualified charity. The amount of the deduction must be prorated to reflect the portion of the proceeds that are donated.

      • If eligible for a deduction, determine if your employee is subject to the $200,000 vesting limit

        For security options granted on or after July 1, 2021, your employee is subject to a $200,000 annual vesting limit under section 110(1)(d) if the qualifying person meets both of the following conditions:

        • It is not a CCPC
        • Has, or is part of a consolidated group that has, gross revenues of more than $500 million

    How to calculate a foreign plan if you are a non-resident employer

    When calculating the taxable benefit, the foreign stock options must be converted to the Canadian dollar equivalent.

    You can use the exchange rate (available from the company or the Bank of Canada) in effect on the dates that make up the transaction (acquisition date): the date the shares are paid for and the date when the treasury shares have been transferred to your employee.

    Where the amount paid or to be paid by an employee pursuant to the terms of a stock option agreement is expressed in terms of a foreign currency, it will not compromise your employee's eligibility for the deduction under paragraph 110(1)(d).

  8. Report the benefit on a slip

    If the benefit is taxable, you must report the following amounts on the T4 slip:

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      • CCPC

        • Box 14 - Employment Income (may include other income)
        • Code 38 - Security options benefits
        • Code 39 - If eligible to claim the deduction under 110(1)(d) - 50% of the taxable benefit
        • Code 41 - If eligible to claim the deduction under 110(1)(d.1) - 50% of the taxable benefit

      • Non-CCPC or mutual fund trust

        • Box 14 - Employment Income (may include other income)
        • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
        • Code 38 - Security option benefit
        • Code 39 - If eligible to claim the deduction under 110(1)(d) - 50% of the taxable benefit received from qualified securities
        • Code 39 - If eligible to claim the deduction under 110(1)(d.01) - Additional deduction 50% of the value of the options exercised after May 1, 2006

      You cannot claim the value of the benefit as an expense.

    • Cash: Option 2

      • No stock agreement or phantom stock plan

        • Box 14 - Employment income (may include other income)
        • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
        • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings

      • Employee elects to cash out

        • Box 14 - Employment income (may include other income)
        • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
        • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
        • Code 38 - Security options benefit
        • Code 39 - If eligible to claim the decurity options deduction under 110(1)(d) - 50% of the amount of the taxable benefit received from qualified securities
        • Code 86 - Security options election
          Employee is eligible for the deduction under 110(1)(d) and employer can decide if they will deduct the cash-out

          The employer must decide if they will deduct the cash-out as an expense or if your employee will be allowed to claim the stock option deduction.

          Either the employer or your employee can claim a deduction, not both.

          If the employer chooses not to claim the cash-out as an expense, the employer must make an election to do so under subsection 110(1.1). This is done by entering the total amount of the security option cash-out that the employer elected not to claim as an expense using code 86, "Security options election" in the "Other information" of the T4 slip. Your employee can claim the deduction on their T1 return (under paragraph 110(1)(d)).

          The amount the employer reports using code 86 may be different from the taxable benefit they have to include in your employee's income in box 14 and using code 38.
