Meal expenses

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide a free or subsized meal, an allowance  or a reimbursement  for overtime meals to your employee.

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if any exceptions apply to the benefit

    The benefit may not be taxable if the benefit is in one of the following situations:

    • You provide a meal part of a travel expense to your employee
    • You provide a meal part of a social event or a hospitality function to your employee

    • If the benefit is in one of the above situations, do not continue to the next step.

      Learn more on the above exceptions using the links.

    • If the benefit is not in one of the above situations, continue to Step 2 - Determine if the benefit provided is for a period of at least 36 hours.

  2. Determine if a benefit is taxable

    Generally, if you provide a free meal, an allowance  or a reimbursement  for meal expenses to your employee, the benefit is taxable.

    Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations

    Situation: You provide a free meal to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide a free meal to your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee is working in the area of health personnel, safety or security
    • Free meal is required to remain on duty
    Example

    A hospital maintains a cafeteria on its premises where all of its 230 employees may get meals at no charge during their working hours. The hospital furnishes meals in order to have 120 employees available for emergencies. Each of these employees is, at times, called upon to perform services during the meal period. Although the hospital does not require these employees to remain on the premises, they rarely leave the hospital during their meal period. Since the hospital furnishes meals on its premises to its employees so that more than half of them are available for emergency calls during meal periods, the hospital can exclude the value of these meals from the wages of all of its employees.

    Taxable situation

    If the free meal you provide to your employee do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Situation: You provide an allowance or you reimburse your employee for an overtime meal

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide an allowance or you reimburse your employee for an overtime meal, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Meal expense is reasonable, generally up to $23 including taxes (higher amounts may be reasonable if the meal cost in a location is higher)
    • Your employee works 2 or more hours of overtime immediately before of after scheduled hours of work
    • Overtime is not frequent and is occasional (less than 3 times a week out of 5 days)
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount being reasonable
    • It is not a form of additional remuneration and is not a form of tax avoidance
    Examples
    Example 1

    In anticipation of a project deadline at the end of the week, an employee works 3.5 hours overtime in addition to their normal work hours on Monday and Tuesday evening. The employee's employer provides a meal allowance after 2 hours of overtime. Therefore, the employee will receive a meal allowance for both evenings. Although the employer pays a reasonable meal allowance for 2 hours of overtime or more, the employee actually works 3.5 hours in addition to their normal working hours. In this example, the employee meets both the hour and frequency requirements. The overtime meal allowances the employee receives for these two evenings will not be a taxable benefit.

    Example 2

    An employee is working on a very large project. In order to meet the deadline, the staff must work an additional 2.5 hours of overtime, in addition to their normal work hours, every Tuesday and Thursday evening for the next 6 weeks. The employee's employer will provide all employees who work overtime with a reasonable meal allowance on those evenings. All conditions stated in the policy must be met in order for the reasonable meal allowance to be excluded from the employee's income. In this example, the reasonable meal allowances received by the employee and the staff would not be a taxable benefit, as they did work the required "2 hours or more" of overtime and the overtime is infrequent.

    Example 3

    At the request of their employer, several employees work through their regular meal period 3 or 4 times per week. For doing so, they are each provided with a meal by the employer. In this situation, because of the frequency, the meals could be viewed as being regularly furnished by the employer as a condition of employment and, consequently, the value of the meals constitutes a taxable benefit to the employees.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance or reimbursement you provide to your employee for meal expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Situation: You provide a subsidized meal to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide a subsidized meal to your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • You operate the facility, for example: dining rooms or cafeterias
    • Your employee pays a reasonable charge, for example: the cost of the food, preparation and service
    • You can justify your position regarding the amount being reasonable
    Example

    Today's special in the cafeteria is fish and chips. Bits and Bites is an employer-operated cafeteria where employees can purchase menu items at subsidized rates. In determining the amount to charge, the cafeteria calculated the cost of the food, preparation and service and then added a small mark-up. Items are available to the public at full price. Since, in this example, the employee pays a reasonable charge that covers the cost of the food, its preparation and service, the value of the meal would not be a taxable benefit to the employee. On the other hand, if the cost to the employee were unreasonable, the value of the meal would be required to be included in their income from office or employment.

    Taxable situation

    If the subsidized meal you provide to your employee do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Situation: You provide a meal part of a collective agreement to your employee

    Taxable situation

    Generally, if you provide a meal part of a collective agreement, it is a taxable benefit because the employee receives an economic advantage by not having to purchase the meal.

    For example, collective agreements may contain clauses that entitle your employee to a fixed meal allowance after working a minimum number of hours of overtime with no requirement that the employee actually eat a meal.

    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations.

      Do not continue to next step.

    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to: Step 3 - Calculate the value of the benefit .

  3. Calculate the value of the benefit

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to the fair market value (FMV) of:

    • Total value of the meal expense that is taxable
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip in code 40 and box 14

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.
