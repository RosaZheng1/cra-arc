Housing, utilities and travel expenses - Provided to an employee in special work sites, including prescribed zones

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide a benefit for housing, utilities and travel expenses to your employee who works or lives in a location that is in a special work site, including in a prescribed zone, in connection with or in the course of their office or employment duties.

Steps

  1. Determine if the benefit is considered in a special situation

    The benefit may not be taxable if you provide to your employee is in one of the following special situations:

    • Refer to: Your employee is working in a remote location
    • Refer to: Your employee has a severe or prolonged impairment
    • Refer to: Your employee is a member of the clergy

    It is possible for an employee to work at a location that is both a special work site and a remote work location. However, the benefit can only be excluded from the employee's income once.

    • If the benefit is in one of the above special situation, do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is not in one of the above special situations, continue to Step 2 - Determine if the benefit provided is for a period of at least 36 hours.

  2. Determine if the benefit provided is for a period of at least 36 hours

    The benefit is provided for a period of more than 36 hours and one of the following apply:

    • Your employee had to be away from their principal place of residence because of their employment duties
    • Your employee had to be at the special work site
    Special situation: Fishers and seafarers

    A ship may qualify as a special work site when it is at sea for a period of 36 hours or more. Time at sea may include time spent on board because of:

    • Quarantine
    • Waiting for docking
    • Unloading cargo
    • Immigration or customs clearance

    Your employee may qualify for exemption from tax on the value of, or a reasonable allowance for, board and lodging received while on a ship. If all conditions are met, a ship will qualify as a special work site at any time the employee is on board, whether it is in port or at sea.

    • If the benefit provided is for a period of less than 36 hours, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable

    • If the benefit provided is for a period of more than 36 hours, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 3 - Determine if the duties performed are considered temporary

  3. Determine if the duties performed are considered temporary

    The duties performed are considered temporary if they are performed on a short-term or interim basis.

    Generally, the CRA considers that an employee's duties is temporary if they are unlikely to provide continuous employment for the employee for more than 2 years.

    To determine if the work is considered temporary, the CRA considers the following factors:

    • Nature of the work the employee performs
    • Total estimated duration of a project or a given phase of a project for which the employee is hired
    • Agreed period of time stated in the employment contract or other terms of the employment agreement

    This must be based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific situation.

    • If the duties performed are not considered temporary, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable

    • If the duties performed are considered temporary, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 4 - Determine if your employee has a principal place of residence or a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE)

  4. Determine if your employee has a principal place of residence or a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE)

    You must determine if your your employee has a principal place of residence or a self-contained domestic establishment (SCDE).

    Situations: Conditions not met

    The CRA generally considers that this condition is not met if one of the following applies:

    • Your employee's principal place of residence or their SCDE is not available throughout the period for occupancy by your employee
    • Your employee rents their principal place of residence or their SCDE to another person
    • If the your employee does not have a principal place of residence or a SCDE, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable

    • If your employee has a principal place of residence or a SCDE, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 5 - Determine if your employee cannot reasonably be expected to travel between their principal place of residence or a SCDE and the special work site

  5. Determine if your employee cannot reasonably be expected to travel between their principal place of residence or a SCDE and the special work site

    The CRA generally considers that it is not reasonable for an employee to travel between their principal place of residence and the workplace if the distance between those two places by the most direct route normally taken is at least 80 kilometres.

    Even if the distance is less than 80 kilometres, the CRA may still consider that it is not reasonable for the employee to make that trip after taking into account the following factors:

    • Condition of the road taken
    • Available means of transportation
    • Number of hours of work required of the employee
    • Time the employee would have for rest if they returned home every day
    • Employee's general state of physical and mental health
    • Time it takes to travel the distance, and the time of day the travel takes place
    • If the distance is considered reasonable, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable

    • If the distance travelled is considered unreasonable, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 6 - Complete Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site

  6. Complete Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site

    You and your employee must fill out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site, before you can to exclude travel, housing and utilities expenses from your employee's income. You must keep a copy.

    The TD4 requires the exact location of the work site and the distance to your employee's principal place of residence.

    • If you and your employee did not fill out Form TD4, do not continue to next step. Refer to the information for most employees: Determine if a benefit is taxable

    • If you and your employee filled out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site, the benefit may not be taxable if all conditions are met.

      Continue to: Step 7 - Determine if the benefit is taxable

  7. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, travel, housing and utilities expenses you provide or reimburse to your employees are taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you paid an allowance or reimburse your employee, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee was required by employment duties to be away for at least 36 hours (step 2)
    • Your employee's employment duties are considered temporary (step 3)
    • Your employee has a principal place of residence or a SCDE (step 4)
    • Your employee cannot reasonably be expected to travel between their principal place of residence or a SCDE and the special work site (step 5)
    • Form TD4 is completed (step 6)
    • If the benefit is an allowance, it is reasonable

    • If the benefit is for travel expenses:

      • it was for a period during which your employee also received housing and utilities benefits
      • it must relate to travel between the work location and your employee's principal place of residence
      • it must relate to travel between the work location and a location in Canada or in the country in which your employee is working
    Examples

    Example 1 - CRA administrative policy does not apply

    A northern community hires Simon to come teach at the local school. The contract is for 1 year. Simon keeps his apartment for the duration of his contract. Simon receives an allowance for board and lodging.

    The CRA administrative policy does not apply because the work is not temporary.

    The position of "teacher" will exist for as long as the school exists, and it cannot be considered temporary, even though the individual currently in this position has a contract for a limited time.

    The temporary nature of the duties is from the employer's point of view, not the employee's.

    Example 2 - CRA administrative policy applies

    Diane is an engineer who lives and works in Montréal, where she maintains a self-contained domestic establishment. She's superving the initial phase of a project in Toronto. The initial phase is for 2 years. The employer provides Annie with lodging in Toronto. Diane and her employee also filled out Form TD4 Declaration of Exemption - Employment at a Special Work Site. Employer kept a copy with her records.

    The CRA administrative policy applies based on the following conditions:

    • Work is temporary
    • Employee maintained a self-contained domestic establishment in Montréal for her personal use
    • Diane has to stay in Toronto for over 36 hours
    • Form TD4 is completed and employer kept a copy

    Example 3 - Income Tax Act applies

    Peter works at a special work site and receives an allowance of $150 per day for board and lodging. This allowance is exempt from income tax under 6(6) of the ITA. On weekends, Peter does not stay on the work site and his employee gives him a check-out allowance of $300. This allowance is equal to the allowance the employee waives when he leaves the site. (2 X $150 per day)

    The Income Tax Act applies because the check-out allowance is reasonable.

    Example 4 - CRA administrative policy does not apply

    Mary works as a crew member of the Pearl. Because the vessel is undergoing repairs, Mary is off work for three weeks. However, her employer is allowing her to remain on board, free of charge, while the repairs are being completed.

    The conditions are not met because the vessel is not at sea. The employer must add the FMV of the meals and lodging (stay on board the ship) to Mary's income.

    Taxable situation

    If the benefit does not meet all of the above conditions, the benefit is taxable.

    • If the benefit is not related to housing expenses, continue to: Step 9 - Calculate the value of the benefit
    • If the benefit is for housing expenses, continue to: Step 8 - Determine if the prescribed zone that contains the employee’s place of residence or work have a developed rental market

  8. Determine if the benefit is considered paid in a prescribed zone

    Select one of the following province or territory to determine if the special work site is located in a prescribed zone:

    • Alberta
    • British Columbia
    • Manitoba
    • Newfoundland and Labrador
    • Nova Scotia
    • Ontario
    • Quebec
    • Saskatchewan
    • Yukon, Nunavut, and the Northwest Territories
    • If the special work site is not located in a prescribed zone, continue to Step 10 - Calculate the value of the benefit
    • If the special work site is located in a prescribed zone, continue to Step 9 - Determine if the prescribed zone that contains the employee’s place of residence or work have a developed rental market

  9. Determine if the prescribed zone that contains the employee’s place of residence or work have a developed rental market

    If the employee resides or works in a prescribed zone, the following cities have a developed market:

    • Dawson Creek, British Columbia
    • Fort McMurray, Alberta
    • Fort St. John, British Columbia
    • Grande Prairie, Alberta
    • Labrador City, Newfoundland
    • Thompson, Manitoba
    • Yellowknife, Northwest Territories
    • Walbush, Newfoundland
    • Whitehorse, Yukon

  10. Calculate the value of the benefit

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to:

    • Fair market value of the benefit received or enjoyed
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value for the travel expenses to be included on the T4 slip using code 30 or 40

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Calculate: Special work sites that is not in prescribed zones

    Add example here.

    • Total value for the board, lodging and transportation expenses
    • minus Value for the board and lodging expenses to be included on the T4 slip using code 30 and box 14
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value for the transportation expenses to be included on the T4 slip using code 40 and box 14
    Calculate: Prescribed zones that has developed rental markets

    If there is an established housing market in the prescribed zone where the employee lives, the value of the benefit related to the housing or utilities you provide the employee corresponds to the fair market value (FMV) of the housing or the FMV of the public utilities, including the GST/HST.

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to the fair market value (FMV) of:

    • Total value of the travel, housing or ustility expenses
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of suitability of size (step 3)
    • minus Value eligible for the reduction amount in respect of loss of privacy and quiet enjoyment (step 3)
    • minus Amount of the travel assistance that is not taxable (to be included on the employee's T4 slip in box 31)
    • equals Value of the travel assistance that is taxable
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Amount of the travel assistance that is taxable (to be included on the employee's T4 slip in boxes 14 and 32)
    Calculate: Accommodations you own - Prescribed zones that do not have developed rental markets

    In places in prescribed zones without developed rental markets, you have to use other methods to set a value on the housing benefit. The method you use depends on whether you own the residence or rent it from a third party.

    If you provide both rent and utilities and can calculate their cost as separate items, you have to determine their value separately. Add both items to get the value of the housing benefit.

    If your employee reimburses you for all or part of their rent or utilities, determine the benefit as explained below. Subtract any amount reimbursed by your employee and include the amount that remains in their income.

    Ceiling amounts for housing benefits

    The ceiling amount for housing benefits is the maximum amount you can include in an employee's income if the employee does not pay any expenses related to housing or utilities.

    The following table shows the monthly ceiling for housing benefits (including GST/HST) for the years 2023 and 2022, by type of housing and utilities.

    Monthly ceiling for housing benefits
    Type of housing Description of services 2023 2022
    Common shelter N/A $227 $213
    Apartment and duplex Rent only $612 $575
    Utilities only $297 $279
    Rent and utilities $910 $855
    House and trailer Rent only $1,024 $962
    Utilities only $452 $425
    Rent and utilities $1,476 $1,387

    If you own a residence that you provide rent free to your employee, report as a benefit whichever of the following amounts is less:

    • Fair market value of the rent
    • Ceiling amount

    Similarly, the amount you have to report as a benefit for utilities is whichever of the following amounts is less:

    • Fair market value of the utilities
    • Ceiling amount
    Example 1 - Travel assistance calculations

    Employer paid an employee $2,000 for them to take a trip from the special work site to their principal place of residence and back in Ontario. Employer paid an employee $850 to assist them to make a personal shopping trip from the special work site in a prescribed zone to a different nearby community on a different trip. The special work site was located 27 kilometres from a town with a population of 43,000 people. Employer and employee completed Form TD4, Declaration of Exemption – Employment at a Special Work Site

    • $2,850 ($2,000 + $850) is the total value of the travel assistance
    • minus $2,000 is the amount of the travel assistance that is not taxable (to be included on the employee's T4 slip in box 31)
    • equals $850 is the value of the travel assistance that is taxable
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $850 is the amount of the travel assistance that is taxable to be included the employee's T4 slip (box 14 and box 32)

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Example 2 - Housing and utilities calculations

    An employer provides an employee with board and lodging in a prescribed zone without a developed rental market with a fair market value of $4,000. The accommodations were owned by the employer. The ceiling value of the board and lodging was $3,000.

    • $4,000 is the total value of the board and lodging
    • minus $1,000 is the amount for the difference between the fair market value and the cleiling amount that is not taxable (to be included on the employee's T4 slip in box 31)
    • equals $3,000 is the value of the board and lodging that is taxable
    • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
    • equals $3,000 is the amount of the board and lodging that is taxable to be included the employee's T4 slip (box 14 and box 32)

    The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    Calculate: Accommodations you rent from a third party - Prescribed zonesthat do not have developed rental markets

    If you rent a residence from a third party and provide it rent free to your employee, report as a benefit whichever of the following amounts is less:

    • Amount you pay the third party
    • Ceiling amount

    Similarly, the amount you have to report as a benefit for utilities is whichever of the following amounts is less:

    • Amount you pay the third party
    • Ceiling amount
