Shareholder benefit

You may need to determine if a person received a shareholder benefit for the purposes of subsection 15(1) of the Income Tax Act.

Before you begin - You must confirm if this content applies to you

This content only applies to persons who are one of the following:

  • Shareholders
  • Are members of a partnership that is a shareholder
  • Are contemplated shareholders
  • Who are not at arm's length with a shareholder of the corporation that gave them a benefit

It also only applies to persons who received a benefit and had knowledge they received a benefit before there was an issue with the CRA.

The content may not apply to situations where the person is deemed to have received a shareholder benefit, including a benefit that is:

  • An acquisition of shares from a division of a non-resident corporation
  • A corporate loan to a shareholder or connected person
  • A corporate loan to an individual due to employment or services
  • A directed to a third party by the employee

The content also may not apply to exceptions under subsection 15(1), which includes benefits that are:

  • Dividends or stock dividend
  • Rights to purchase shares that are identical to the rights given to all other shareholders
  • Given by the reduction of the paid-up capital of the corporation
  • Given by the redemption, acquisition, or cancellation of shares
  • Given on the winding-up or reorganization of the corporation

Type of benefit

Q1. What is the type of shareholder benefit?

  • Use of corporate tangible property (e.g. real estate, car, horse, parking space)
  • A payment or reimbursement of expenses
  • A life insurance policy or personal health services plan
  • A transaction involving the corporation from which the employee profited
  • Stock options or discretionary dividend shares
  • Funds
  • Other type of benefit

Use of the corporate property

Q1a. Did the employee or employee's family use the corporate property exclusively for business?

  • Yes (Example: Employee only used a corporate limousine to entertain clients)
  • No (Example: Employee used a corporate limousine to entertain clients and used it at night for personal purpose)

Q1b. Was the corporation's primary reason for purchasing or renting the property to personally benefit the employee or the employee's family?

  • Yes (Example: Corporation bought a cottage to give the employee a place to sleep)
  • No (Example: Corporation bought a corporate limousine to entertain clients and gave the employee permission to use the limousine as well)

Nature of the benefit

Q2. Did the corporation receive fair market value for the benefit from the employee or the employee's family?

  • Yes (Example: Benefit was personal use of corporate property and the employee paid fair market value for their personal use of the property)
  • No

Q3. Did the corporation give the benefit to pay off a debt that the corporation owed to the employee?

  • Yes (Example: Employee provides the business $10,000 for the business to pay their workers and the business paid the employee a $1,000 bonus each year in repayment of that debt)
  • No (Example: Amount of the debt was not adjusted in the corporate books or shareholder loan account at the time the benefit was conferred)

Q3a. Was the value of the benefit given to the taxpayer greater than the amount the corporation owed to the employee?

  • Yes (Example: Business owed the employee $30,000.00 but paid the employee $50,000.00)
  • No (Example: Business gave the employee a vehicle worth $22,500.00)

Q4. Did any customers or clients of the corporation receive the same benefit?

  • Yes (Example: Employee received a voucher for a free movie and certain clients of the company also received the same vouchers)
  • No (Example: Employee received a payment for a loan held with the corporation)

Q5. Did the employee own 50% or more of the corporation’s outstanding shares?

  • Yes (Example: Employee was the sole owner of the corporation and owned 100% of the shares in the corporation)
  • No (Example: Employee only owned 49% of the shares in the corporation)

Q6. Was the benefit given pursuant to a formal, written agreement with the corporation?

  • Yes (Example: Corporation paid rent to the employee for the use of the employee’s premises in accordance with a rental agreement entered into by both the corporation and the employee)
  • No

Q7. Was the benefit given to entertain clients or potential clients?

  • Yes (Example: Corporation paid for the employee’s ticket to a basketball game for the employee to go with clients of the business)
  • No

Q8. Was the benefit solely used to maintain or obtain a client, contract, or asset in furtherance of the business?

  • Yes (Example: Corporation paid for the employee’s travel expenses for the taxpayer to secure a lucrative contract for the corporation)
  • No (Example: Corporation purchased an asset from the employee for more than fair market value and did not have a legitimate business objective in paying more than fair market value)

Q9. Was the benefit given to reimburse the cost of professional fees incurred by the employee?

  • Yes (Example: Employee incurred lawyer fees to prevent the corporation from temporarily halting operations and the corporation reimbursed the employee for such fees)
  • No

Conferral of the benefit

Q10. Was the benefit related to the performance of the employee’s duties at the corporation as a worker, director or officer?

  • Yes (Example: Benefit was given to the employee to perform their duties in this role)
  • No (Example: Employee had no role at the corporation other than as a shareholder)

Q11. Was the benefit given to the employee pursuant to a worker agreement with the corporation?

  • Yes (Example: Benefit was given to the employee through their role as an independent contractor at the corporation)
  • No (Example: Employee worked as an independent contractor at the corporation but their agreement with the corporation did not mention the benefit at issue)

Q12. Was the same benefit offered to any workers, officers or directors of the corporation?

  • Yes (Example: At least one worker, officer or director received the same benefit as the employee in their capacity as worker, officer or director)
  • No (Example: Benefit was only given to the employee)

Factors indicating that the employee is the primary beneficiary

Many situations when the employee is the primary beneficiary, the benefit is not tied to the employee's specific employment duties. The benefit is believed to enhance the duties of the job by the employee, but not the employer.

  • There was personal use of the benefit
  • No restrictions on the ability of the employee to use the benefit for personal use
  • No limit on the ability of the benefit to be used for personal use
  • The benefit does not affect or improves employee's work productivity
  • The employee would of used or purchased the benefit regardless of their specific duties
  • It is not common in the industry to provide the benefit as it has little or no effect on performace of duties
  • An employee's relative or friend used the benefit
  • The benefit is given to other employees with different duties
  • The benefit is only used once

Factors indicating that the employer is the primary beneficiary

Many situations when the employer is the primary beneficiary, the benefit is tied to the employee's specific employment duties. There are also indicators that the job duties may not be possible without the benefit.

  • No personal use of the benefit
  • Restrictions were placed on the use of benefit to avoid personal use
  • The nature of the benefit was meant for business use
  • The benefit improves the employee's work productivity
  • The job duties being fulfilled would not be possible without the benefit
  • It is common in the industry to provide the benefit to allow employees to more effectively perform their duties
  • The employee recieved the benefit
  • The benefit is not given to other employees, and is tied to a specific employee duties
  • The benefit can be used on an ongoing basis

Analyze the facts

You must analyze the information from both the employee and the employer to help you decide if the evidence moves the employee or the employer is the primary beneficiary of the benefit side of the continuum.

Do the facts indicate that, for the benefit, the:

  • Employee is the primary beneficiary
  • Employer is the primary beneficiary

You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will move the worker into one side of the continuum, and confirm if the employee or the employer is the primary beneficiary of the benefit.

Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support the benefit is taxable or not.

Case-by-case basis

Whether the employee or the employer is the primary beneficiary of the benefit needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to move the employee or the employer as the primary beneficiary of the benefit to one side of the continuum or the other, you must examine all factors. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

If the primary beneficiary of the benefit is the employee, you will need to determine if the benefit is taxable. Learn more: Determine if a benefit is taxable.

What if you cannot confirm who is the primary beneficiary

You can ask for a CPP/EI ruling. If you are not sure of who is the primary beneficiary (employer or employee), you can request a CPP/EI ruling.

Learn more: How to get a CPP/EI ruling
