Tax obligations: Payer or self-employed worker relationship

If you are not sure if the worker is an employee, continue to: Deciding the employment status

Understanding the tax obligations based on the individual:

  • Payer

Payer

Understanding the payer's tax obligations:

  • Payroll obligations
  • Income tax obligations
  • GST/HST obligations
  • Other tax obligations

Payroll obligations

Generally, any person must open a payroll account, calculate and remit (pay) any income tax deductions withheld, and file information returns if they make payments to a self-employed worker.

Withholding and remitting deductions

Generally, the payer does not withhold or remit income tax, EI, CPP from the payment made to a self-employed worker.

Under special EI regulations, the payer must remit EI on behalf of the self-employed worker if the worker is a:

  • Fisher
  • Barber or hairdresser
  • Taxi or other passenger vehicle driver

Learn more: Get ready to make deductions

If the payer makes a payment in a self-employment relationship, the payer must report on the following slip depending on who the payment is made to:

  • T4 slip

    Self-employed fisher, barber, hairdresser, taxi driver, or a driver of other passenger vehicle

  • T4A slip

    Self-employed farmers, recipients of fees for services and other self-employed workers

Learn more: Payroll

Income tax obligations

The payer must report and pay tax on all income earned from taxable activities. The income is the net earnings from employment, property, investments and benefits. To report and pay tax, the payer must calculate the business income and expenses using one of the following forms when filing their income tax return:

  • T1 return (business income)
    • Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities
    • Form T2042, Statement of Farming Activities
    • Form T2121, Statement of Fishing Activities

    Learn more: Lines 13499 to 14300 – Self-employment income

  • T2 return (corporate income)

    Schedule 125, Income Statement Information

    Learn more: Completing your corporation income tax return

  • T5013 return (partnership income)

    Schedule 125, Income Statement Information

    Learn more: T5013 Partnership Information Return filing requirements

Learn more about other income tax obligations:

  • Personal income tax
  • Corporation income tax

GST/HST obligations

Generally, any person must open a GST/HST account, collect, file and remit (pay) the GST/HST if they provide goods or services and earn more than $30,000 over 4 calendar quarters.

Learn more: Collect, file, and remit (pay) the GST/HST

Other tax obligations

Depending on the type of industry, the payer may have other tax obligations, for example:

  • Information returns account (RZ), for businesses that file information returns, such as T5, T5013, and more
  • Registered charity account (RR), for businesses that are charitable organizations
  • Underused housing tax account (RU), for corporations that file an Underused Housing Tax return for each of their properties in Canada.
  • Fuel charge account (CT), for businesses that need to include a fuel charge on products
  • Excise duties account (RD), for businesses that need to charge excise duty on products
  • Excise tax and special levies account (RE), for businesses that need to charge excise tax on products
  • Air travelers security charge account (RG), for air carriers that charge the air travelers security charge
  • Insurance premium tax account (RN), for brokers and agents who place contracts of insurance
  • Luxury tax account (LT), for businesses that need to charge luxury tax on the sale or importation of select luxury items
Self-employed worker

Understanding the self-employed worker's tax obligations:

  • Income tax obligations
  • GST/HST obligations
  • Tax obligations for workers of the self-employed worker
  • Other tax obligations

Income tax obligations

Self-employed workers who are residents of Canada are required to report their worldwide income from all sources.

If the self-employed worker is a non-resident, they are subject to Canadian income tax on most Canadian-sourced income paid or credited to them during the year unless all or part of that income is exempt under a tax treaty.

When filing the T1 return, the self-employed worker uses the amounts from the T4 or T4A slip to:

  • Calculate and report business income and expenses using:

    • Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities
    • Form T2042, Statement of Farming Activities
    • Form T2121, Statement of Fishing Activities

    Learn more: Report business income and expenses

  • Calculate the amount of CPP/QPP contributions because a self-employed worker is responsible for both the employer’s and employee's contributions to the plan using one of the following:

    • Schedule 8, CPP Contributions and Overpayment (for all except QC)
    • Schedule 8, QPP Contributions (for QC only)
    • Form RC381, Inter-Provincial Calculation for CPP and QPP Contributions and Overpayments

    Learn more: Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions for CPP working beneficiaries

  • If the self-employed worker chooses to register for the self-employed EI program, calculate the amount of EI premiums only if the self-employed worker is not a barber, hairdresser, fisher, taxi driver, or a driver of other passenger vehicles using Schedule 13, EI Premiums on Self-Employment and Other Eligible Earnings (under special EI regulations, the payer is responsible to remit EI on behalf of the worker on all of their insurable earnings)

    Learn more: EI benefits for self-employed people

Receiving the slip

Generally, the payer must provide the self-employed worker with a T4A or T4 slip which includes any payment made regardless if the income is taxable or not.

  • Options if the self-employed worker does not expect to get the slip: Get a copy of a slip

GST/HST obligations

Generally, any person must open a GST/HST account, collect, file and remit (pay) the GST/HST if they provide goods or services and earn more than $30,000 over the previous 4 (or fewer) consecutive calendar quarters.

Learn more: Collect, file, and remit (pay) the GST/HST

Tax obligations for workers of the self-employed worker

The tax obligations for workers of the self-employed worker are the same for employees or self-employed workers of regular employers or payers.

Other tax obligations

Depending on the type of industry, the self-employed worker may have other tax obligations, for example:

Keeping records

Individuals must keep track of all of the income that they earned and the expenses that they incurred for business purposes.

Learn more: Keeping records

Help for small businesses

Small businesses that would like help in understanding their tax obligations can ask for a visit from a Canada Revenue Agency Liaison Officer.

Learn more: Liaison Officer service – free tax help for small business owners and self-employed individuals.

Correcting tax affairs

Individuals that would like to correct their tax affairs can apply for relief under the Voluntary Disclosures Program.

Learn more: Voluntary Disclosures Program – Introduction

Consequences of non-compliance

When non-compliance is identified, the CRA:

  • Educates the individual on their tax obligations
  • Ensures the correct returns are prepared and filed
  • Takes the necessary enforcement actions to ensure compliance with the law

Depending on the situation, penalties may be assessed. If individuals do not pay an amount that is due, the CRA may apply interest from the day the payment was due.
