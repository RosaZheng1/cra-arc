Payroll examinations and GST/HST account reviews

The purpose of the payroll examinations and GST/HST account reviews is to maintain the integrity of the tax system with respect to all of the following:

  • The reporting of employment income and taxable benefits
  • The withholding and remitting of payroll related amounts
  • The proper characterization of workers (employee or self-employed)
  • The GST/HST collecting and filing requirements

They are completed through a combination of taxpayer education and responsible enforcement.

Why are examinations and reviews important

The examinations and reviews promotes employer awareness and understanding of tax laws and obligations as provided in the Income Tax Act, the Excise Tax Act, the Canada Pension Plan, the Employment Insurance Act, and their respective regulations, to increase and enhance voluntary compliance.

Under the Canada Pension Plan and the Employment Insurance Act, the CRA is responsible for determining:

  • Whether or not an individual's employment is pensionable under the Canada Pension Plan and/or insurable under the Employment Insurance Act
  • The amount of pensionable and/or insurable earnings
  • Whether or not Canada Pension Plan (CPP) contributions and employment insurance (EI) premiums are payable
  • How many hours an insured person has in insurable employment
  • How long an employment lasts, including the dates on which the employment began and ended
  • The amount of CPP contributions and/or EI premiums payable
  • Who is the employer
  • Whether or not employers are considered to be associated for the purposes of the Employment Insurance Act
  • The refund amount

The CRA is also responsible for ensuring that CPP contributions, EI premiums, and income tax are deducted, remitted, and reported as required by legislation.

  1. Your account is selected for review

    Timeline: Day 1

    The CRA may contact you (or your authorized representative) to inform you that your payroll account  has been selected for review.

    This review helps confirm that income amounts, deductions, and credits are reported correctly and can be properly supported.

    About authorized reprensentative

    Even if a professional tax preparer completed your payrol information returns (for example, T4 slips and T4 summary) on your behalf, you are responsible for making sure your business meets all their payroll tax obligations. Generally, tax preparers will use the information you give them to prepare your remittance amounts and information returns, but they may not be aware of all your tax obligations. This is why the CRA may contact you directly for the review.

    If the CRA sends a letter to the authorized representative asking for information about one of their clients, the authorized representative must respond by the date mentioned in the letter.

    You want to authorize a representative

    You can use different methods to authorize a representative.

    Learn more: Authorize a representative: How to give authorization.

    Methods the CRA may contact you

    By phone

    If the CRA calls you (or your authorized representative), the CRA officer assigned to your case will identify themselves when they contact you by phone. They will provide you with the following:

    • Their name and their phone number
    • Your case or payroll account  number
    • The purpose of the review
    Doubt about the caller

    If you doubt the caller's identity, make sure the caller is a CRA employee before providing any information over the phone. You can also wait until you receive the review appointment confirmation letter before providing additional information.

    Do not verify the phone number during the call or rely on the number displayed on your caller ID. End the call and verify the callback number to confirm if a phone number is from the CRA.

    Confirm a phone number is from the CRA

    Information the CRA may ask you if you decide to call the CRA

    To verify your identity, the CRA may ask for:

    • Your social insurance number
    • Your full name
    • Your date of birth
    • Your complete address
    • The amount entered at line 15000 off your most recent notice of assessment
    Send you a letter

    If the CRA sends you (or your authorized representative) a letter, the following information will be included:

    • The name of CRA officer assigned to the case and their phone number
    • Your case or payroll account  number
    • The purpose of the review
    • The timeline for the next step of the review
    Online - Email notification

    You (or your authorized representative) may receive an email notification from the CRA if you signed up to receive email notifications.

    • Form: PD7A, Statement of account for current source deductions

    For security reasons, the CRA officer cannot contact you directly by email.

    Collaborating with the CRA officer assigned to your case

    Once you are satisfied regarding the identity of the CRA officer, the CRA recommends that you answer the officers’s questions to the best of your ability. Collaborating with the officer will lead to a smooth and efficient review.

    Your rights during the review

    The CRA operates on the fundamental belief that you are more likely to comply with the law if you have the information and other services that you need to meet your obligations.

    The CRA wants to make sure you receive all of your entitlements and that you understand and can exercise your rights.

  2. Gather your documents and prepare for the review

    Timeline: Within 1 month

    Make sure that your records are accurate, complete, and organized to facilitate a smooth review process.

    A CRA officer will usually contact you by phone to begin the review process. The officer will also send you a second letter confirming the date and the time to review your books and records. If an in-person examination is required, we will also provide you the address for this review.

    Normally, the CRA officer will work at the place of business. Occasionally, the CRA officer will work at an alternate location, such as your accountant’s office, or borrow documents to complete the review at a CRA office. The CRA officer will make arrangements to speak with you to discuss any questions that arise during the review.

    Types of books and records the CRA may review
    Accounts receivable and payable (ledgers and journals)

    These records provide:

    • Information regarding your ability to pay
    • A list of assets to assist collections with third party demands (garnishment)
    Bank deposit book

    Provides information on your accounts receivable (garnishment)

    Bank statements, cancelled cheques, cheque register

    Provides information on your ability to pay (for example: cash flow, balance, amount of payments and withdrawals), including:

    • Verification for information in the salary and wage journal (for example: dates, amounts, endorsements)
    • Proof of payment where remittances may be missing from the CRA systems
    • Information that may indicate payments which should have been included in payroll but were not (for example: taxable benefits, casual workers)
    Cash disbursements journal

    These records including the petty cash journal may provide:

    • Information that may indicate payments which should have been included in the payroll but were not (for example: taxable benefits, casual workers)
    • Information that may lead to a referral to another CRA team (for example: possible referral to Rulings)
    Cash receipts journal

    The cash receipts journal is used to keep track of cash received and shows the accounts affected by the cash receipts

    Current financial statements

    The current balance sheet and income statement will provide:

    • An overall view of the profitability, extent of operations, net worth, assets and liabilities
    • Salaries, wages, management salaries and sub-contract costs expensed
    • Owner's equity and shareholders loans
    General ledger and general journal

    This ledger/journal provides information such as:

    • A summary of all of your financial record-keeping
    • Information pertaining to shareholder loan accounts, vehicles, equipment, and inventory
    Minute book and share register

    These records provide:

    • Correct name of corporation
    • Type of charter held, for example, federal or provincial
    • The minutes of shareholders meetings
    • The name and addresses of the shareholders and directors
    • The number of shares held by each shareholder (this helps to establish the percentage of the voting shares controlled by each shareholder)
    • Details of transfers where share holdings have changed since incorporation
    Petty cash account

    Record of cash payment made by your business. It is reviewed to identify potential unreported income.

    Salary/wage journal (manual or computerized)

    This journal provides:

    • Gross and net amounts paid to employees, including overtime pay, incentive bonuses, and more
    • Amounts deducted and/or to be paid for tax, CPP and EI
    • Pay period dates and pay dates
    • Amounts to be remitted to the Receiver General
    • Dates when remittances are due
    • Personal employee or other workers information

    When reviewing the salary/wage journal, the CRA reviews the:

    • Province in which the employee reports for work
    • Calculation of gross salary including overtime pay, and incentive bonuses
    Synoptic journal
    • A summary of deposits, revenues, cash transactions, and cheques prepared on a periodic basis
    • Classifies the revenues and expenses into various categories
    TD1 - Personal Tax Credits Return

    The CRA reviews the TD1 to ensure that:

    • It is signed within a reasonably current date
    • Claims for exemptions and tuition fees are reasonable
    • The individuals are actually employees of the business
    • Each employee has a social insurance number

    All provinces and territories have their own personal tax credits return that must also be completed in order to withhold correct amount of provincial tax.

    TD1X - Statement of Commission Income and Expenses for Payroll Tax Deductions

    Employees who are paid in whole, or in part, by commission can choose to complete this form.

    The CRA reviews the TD1X to ensure that:

    • Rate of tax to be deducted from commission income
    • Employee's prior year income and expenses or the estimated amounts for the current year
    • Employee's estimated annual taxable income
    • Employee's personal information, for example, address and social insurance number
    • A copy of employee's signature for comparison purposes, for example, to verify endorsement of cancelled cheques
    TD3F - Fisher's Election to have Tax Deducted at Source

    When a fisher sells a catch, the fisher can elect to have the buyer, also known as the designated employer, deduct tax from the proceeds of the sale.

    The CRA reviews the TD3F to ensure that:

    • Fisherman's personal information, for example, address and social insurance number
    • A copy of the fisherman's signature for comparison purposes, for example, to verify endorsement of cancelled cheques
    T4 information return - Summary of Remuneration Paid

    The CRA reviews the T4 information return to ensure that:

    • Compare a random sample of the gross salary or wages, taxable allowances and benefits and registered pension plan deductions reported to the amounts shown in the salary and wage journal
    • Reconcile the total wages for the year as recorded in the salary and wage journal with the total wages reported on the summary form
    • Reconcile the total deductions for the year as recorded in the salary and wage journal to the amount of the T4 slip
    • Ensure that bonus payments and amounts paid after the end of the calendar year are reported by the recipient as income in the year they were received, paying close attention to any amended returns filed after the filing date
    • Ensure that all taxable benefits and other income are reported
    T4A information summary - Summary of Pension, Retirement Annuity, and Other Income

    The CRA reviews the T4A information return to ensure that:

    • Tax deductions from pension and other income which should have been remitted to the Receiver General
    • Gross pension and other income for all of the payees
    • Pension and other income and tax deductions for each payee
    • Names, addresses and social insurance numbers of all the recipients of pension and other income
    • Total commissions received by a self-employed worker
    • Pension adjustment
    Work in progress

    You should be able to provide a list of current contracts and orders outstanding including size, due date and expected delivery of goods or services.

    Examples of documents you may need to send to the CRA

    The following are examples of documents you may need to send to the CRA:

    • General ledger
    • Subsidiary ledgers
    • Year-end adjusting entries
    • Payroll journals that show salaries, wages, and commissions for the period (manual or electronic)
    • List of subcontractors and self-employed individuals and their contact information, copies of contracts and invoices
    • Cash receipts and disbursements journal
    • Shareholders loan journal
    • Corporation documents (minute book, director and shareholders registry)
    • Bank statements, cancelled cheques and cheque register
    • List of current accounts receivable (including credit card and merchant numbers) and accounts payable
    • TD1-Personal tax credits return
    • TD1X-Statment of commision income and expenses for payroll tax deductions
    • TD3F-Fisher's election to have tax deducted at source
    • T4 slips and T4 summary - For employers
    • T4A slips and T4A summary - For payers
    • List of all the taxable benefits for all employees, shareholders, directors, and other person(s)
    • Automobile lease and purchase agreements, including contracts and logbooks
    • Most recent financial statements
    • Completed Form T2125, Statement of Business or Professional Activities
    • List of assets
    • Outstanding GST/HST returns for the specific periods
    • Outstanding sales invoices, purchase invoices and summaries for specific periods

    In addition to the above documents, the CRA may ask for input from your accountant, book-keeper, and/or employees, about information that relates to the books and records and what was reported in the information returns.

    Verify the identity of the CRA officer

    Phone call review
    In-person review

    The authorized officer will carry a CRA authorization card (RC121A) to prove that they are a representative of the CRA. The CRA authorization card includes the employee's name and badge number. It is the primary method of employee identification.

    Doubt about the CRA officer

    If you doubt the legitimacy of a visit from a CRA officer, you can contact the CRA and ask an agent to confirm the officer's identity.

  3. Sending your documents for the review

    Timeline: Within 1 month

    The second contact letter will request specific payroll documents, including the deadline to submit your documents. Once you receive this request, you are legally required to provide the requested books and records for this review.

    If your books and records are available electronically, the CRA officer may ask for your electronic records before meeting with you. This usually speeds up the review process and leads to less time spent at your place of business.

    If electronic records are not available, or physical records are required, the CRA officer will either:

    • Review the documents at your place of business or representative’s office.
    • Borrow the documents that are required for further review. In this case, the CRA officer will provide you with a detailed receipt for any borrowed documents.
      What if the CRA borrows your books and records

      To borrow your books and records, the CRA officer will:

      • Complete a T2213 receipt identifying the borrowed books, records and documents
      • Provide you with a copy of the receipt

      When the documents are returned, the CRA officer will request that you sign on the receipt acknowledging you received the books and records.

    Generally, you have 30 days from the date of this letter to provide the documents. You can ask for an extention to the CRA officer assigned to your case.

    Documents sent by the authorized reprensentative

    You must send the name and phone number of an authorized person to contact in case we need more information.

    You can use different methods to authorize a representative.

    Learn more: Authorize a representative: How to give authorization.

  4. CRA review of your documents

    Timeline: Within 3 to 12 months

    The time it takes to complete a payroll examination review varies. There may be delays if you cannot provide books and records as requested. If you no longer have certain records, speak with the officer assigned to your case to find alternate methods to confirm the amounts reported on the information returns.

    There may also be delays caused by the CRA. For example, the officer may need to consult other CRA tax specialists or the officer may be absent for personal reasons. In some cases, it may also be necessary for the CRA to assign a new officer to the payroll examination review.

  5. When the review is done - Your detailed results

    Timeline: Within X month

    After the review is completed, the CRA may send you (as the employer) all of the following:

    • A letter inform you if you (as the employer) are fully compliant or if any discrepancies were found that need to be corrected.

      If the CRA officer finds that no changes need to be made to the information returns, the officer will close the case.

      You have 30 days from the date of your letter to respond to the letter.

    • A PD83-1, Payroll examination Statement of Account showing the result of the examination and the balance owing (if applicable).

    • A notice of reassessment (NOR) showing any changes to your information return (for example, if you under-remitted or significant errors are found). The NOR will detail the additional amounts owed, including any penalties or interest applied to overdue payments. It will also provide instructions on how to make the payment and the due date.

      You have 30 days from the date of your NOR to pay your balance owing.

    What if you disagree

    You may agree or disagree with the proposed results. If you disagree with the changes proposed by the officer, you are encouraged to contact the officer to try and resolve disagreements. If issues remain unresolved, you can contact the officer’s team leader to discuss further.

  6. Follow-up review notice

    Timeline: Within 3 months

    If the CRA determines that further review is necessary, you may receive a notice of a follow-up review. This letter will outline the scope of the next review and the records you need to prepare for the next stage of second review.

  7. Your right to appeal if you disagree

    Timeline: Varies depending on the case

    If you want to dispute your reassessment, you have the right to object. When you file an objection, you have to explain why you disagree and include all relevant facts and supporting documents. Once the CRA makes a decision on your objection, you must pay any remaining amounts you owe, including taxes, penalties, and interest. Note that, in most cases, interest charges still apply to an amount owing while it is in dispute.

    If you disagree with the CRA's review, you will be sent a formal letter regarding your right to appeal. This letter will outline the process for filing a Notice of Objection or appealing the reassessment to the Tax Court of Canada.
