Payroll examination and account review process

Before the payroll review

1. Initial contact letter

This letter notifies the employer that their payroll records have been selected for review. The letter will outline the purpose of the review, the records required, and the timeframe for the examination.

2. Request for documents

The CRA will send a follow-up request for specific payroll records such as payroll ledgers, employee timesheets, T4 summaries, and remittance forms. This letter will typically include a deadline for submission of the documents.

3. Confirmation of scheduled review

The CRA may send a confirmation letter outlining the date and time of the review if an in-person examination is required. This letter will confirm the CRA auditor assigned to the case and the format of the review (onsite or virtual).

After the payroll review

4. Review findings report

After the review, the CRA will send a detailed report outlining the findings. This report will indicate whether the employer is fully compliant or if any discrepancies were found that need to be corrected.

5. Notice of reassessment (NOR)

If under-remittances or significant errors are found, the CRA will issue a Notice of Reassessment. This document will detail the additional amounts owed, including any penalties or interest applied to overdue payments.

6. Penalties and interest notice

If the CRA identifies non-compliance with remittance schedules or payroll errors, you may receive a letter outlining any penalties and interest charges. This notice will provide instructions on how to make the payment and the due date.

7. Follow-up audit notice

If the CRA decides that further review is necessary, you may receive a notice of a follow-up audit. This letter will outline the scope of the next audit and the records you need to prepare for the next stage of examination.

8. Appeal or objection letter

If you disagree with the CRA's findings, you will be sent a formal letter regarding your right to appeal. This letter will outline the process for filing a Notice of Objection or appealing the reassessment to the Tax Court of Canada.
