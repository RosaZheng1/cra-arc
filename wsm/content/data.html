Do your own keyword research

Use Google Trends to show search term frequency by country, province, timeframe, and category.

Example: Comparison of "wellness" versus "well-being"

In the 12 months, searches in Canada for "wellness" out-performs "well-being". Google provides the data based on a scale of 100. The daily average is:

  • 84 searches for "wellness"
  • 1 search for "well-being"
Example: Comparison of "procurement" versus "purchasing"

In the 12 months, searches in Canada for "procurement" is similar to searches for "purchasing". Google provides the data based on a scale of 100.

However:

  1. Scroll down the page to get more information.
  2. Change the drop-down "Rising" to "Top" to see the context of how procurement is most frequently searched.
    Before

  3. Notice the context of when "procurement" is being searched. The third most popular "procurement" search is "procurement meaning", which suggests that many users don't know what it is in the first place. So although "procurement" is a popular term, it is not necessarily popular for the right reason.

Determining a user's keywords is important, but be sure to also determine why it's popular. After a thorough analysis, popular keywords must be considered when creating content.

Many forces pressure Web writers to diminish a website's value by filling it with words that are unlikely to appear in search queries. Here are some guidelines for writing to ensure that users will find your site:

  • Supplement made-up words with known words
  • Play down marketese and internal vocabulary
  • Supplement brand names with generic terms
  • Avoid "politically correct" terminology

Familiar words spring to mind when users create their search queries. If your writing favors made-up terms over legacy words, users won't find your site.

Get data from existing GC sources

There are many sources of data available when you are designing content.

Online data sources
  • Web analytics to find out how often people visit the page, how much time they spend on a page and where they go from there
  • Search terms and searches from the page can tell you what people expect to find, but can’t web page feedback collected on each page
  • How people are using Canada.ca - basic data
  • Government of Canada 100 top tasks inventory
  • Social listening - using your topic keywords to browse conversations on social networks and forums like Twitter, Facebook, Reddit
  • Metadata audit tool to find any problems related to how content is found in search
Call-in data sources
  • Call centre data
  • Complaints and feedback received from the public
Engagment sessions
  • Public Opinion Research studies and other research studies funded by your organization; some reports such as adults and evaluation reports are available on Canada.ca institutional pages, for example Canada Revenue Agency reports)
  • User research and usability testing done by your or other government organizations
  • Data from content improvement projects on Canada.ca
