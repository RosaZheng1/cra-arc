Refund on your tax return

If you have an amount on line 48400 of your personal tax return, you may receive a refund.

You must wait for your tax return to be processed by the CRA to confirm that you have a refund.

Why you may not receive a refund

Generally, you will not receive a refund if your refund amount is $2 or less.

The CRA may keep all or part of your refund if you have:

  • An amount owing (or pending amount owing)
  • A garnishment order under the Family Orders and Agreements Enforcement Assistance Act
  • Certain other outstanding federal, provincial or territorial government debts, such as student loans, employment insurance (EI) and social assistance benefit overpayments, immigration loans or training allowance overpayments
  • Any outstanding GST/HST returns from a sole proprietorship or partnership

For more information, refer to: How payments are applied to offset debt

Processing your refund

Steps

  1. Wait for processing

    How you file your tax return will affect when you get your refund.

    As long as you send your return on or before the due date, we will process it and typically send you your refund within the following timelines:

    • If you filed online

      Processing time: Within 2 weeks

    • If you filed by paper

      Processing time: Within 8 weeks

    Non-resident refunds will typically take 16 weeks to process, regardless of how the return is filed.

  3. Get your refund

    You can choose to have your refund sent to you:

    • By direct deposit

      If you have direct deposit, we deposit your refund directly into your bank account.

      If you do not have direct deposit, refer to: Set up your direct deposit

    • By cheque

      If your notice of assessment includes a cheque, you can cash the cheque at any time.

    Interest on your refund

    In some situations, the CRA will pay you compound daily interest on your 2023 tax refund.

    The calculation will start on the latest of the following 3 dates:

    • May 30, 2024
    • The 30th day after you file your return
    • The day you overpaid your taxes

    For more information, refer to: Prescribed interest rates.
